## Notes & context
★★★ **"Since the army was broken" — the Rhins mustering after Dunbar.** The presbytery's formal **"advice to the Committee of the Shire of Wigton"** (given at Glenluce, 16 Sept 1650, and copied into the register) is the local machinery of re-mobilisation after **Dunbar**: it urges the committee **"to lay aside other motions that might retard the mustering of the forces," to hasten in every man still owing to the Committee of Association "in this extraordinary strait of the land,"** and — explicitly — **"all deficients of former levies … since the army was broken, should be hastened up with all possible speed and go along with the forces next to go out,"** with gentlemen and well-affected to lead by example. The phrase **"since the army was broken"** is a plain reference to the shattering of the Covenanter host at Dunbar (3 Sept). This is the "Committee of Association" (the Western Association's war committee) and the Shire acting to rebuild the forces.

★★ **Malignants returned from Ireland — and a war-crime allegation.** The Ireland thread (imgs 204–205) sharpens into named cases across the North Channel: **John ("Captain") Campbell** is accused (on **Knockeghinnoch's** information) of being **"one of the number who apprehended him in Ireland to range him to George Munro, and inhumanely beat him all the way"** — i.e. of forcibly conscripting men in Ulster for Munro's army; he denies it, and **John Adair of Maryport** (an eyewitness) is to be examined. **Robert Campbell**, lately of the **parish of Donaghadee** (the Ulster port opposite Portpatrick), must produce a testimonial of his conduct there — he has only a paper "subscribed by several inhabitants of Donaghadee," referred for verification. These entries document the tight **Rhins–Ulster (Donaghadee/Portpatrick) traffic** and the Kirk's effort to vet returning men for malignancy.
