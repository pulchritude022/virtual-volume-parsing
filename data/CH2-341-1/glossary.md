# Glossary — Stranraer Presbytery Minutes, 1641–1652

Scots, Latin, and ecclesiastical-procedural terms that recur across the transcriptions.
Parsed by `scripts/build_site.py` from this file's `##` headings — add a term by adding a
heading here; no other file needs to change.

## Presbytery

The court of Presbyterian church government sitting above the individual kirk session and
below the Synod — a regional body of ministers (and, for some business, ruling elders) from
several neighbouring parishes, meeting regularly (usually at Stranraer in this volume) to
handle discipline appeals, examine and admit ministers, and administer collections and
correspondence across its bounds.

## Kirk session

The lowest court of church government, run by a parish's minister and its ruling elders,
handling routine local discipline (fornication, Sabbath-breaking, slander) before any escalation
to the presbytery.

## Synod

The Provincial Assembly — here, the **Synod of Galloway** — sitting above the presbytery
and below the General Assembly; the venue for appeals the presbytery could not resolve, and
for revising and "approving" each presbytery's register once a year.

## General Assembly

The national court of the Church of Scotland, the final court of appeal in this system.
Both Mr Gilbert Power's deposition and Alexander McCulloch of Ardwell's slander case were
carried this far.

## Commission of the Kirk

A standing committee delegated by the General Assembly to act with its authority between
Assemblies — the body that, in 1648–49, directs the Covenant-renewal drive and the search
for Engagement compliers, and to which James Ross of Balneil's process is referred.

## Moderator

The minister elected to chair a given sitting of the presbytery (or Synod); the office
rotates, typically re-elected after each Synod meeting.

## Clerk

The minister (occasionally described separately from the moderator) responsible for keeping
the presbytery's written register — the very document this project transcribes. Clerkship
changes hands several times across the volume (Livingstone → George Dick → John Turnbull →
Hew/Hugh Hutcheson, among others), and back-filled entries written by a later clerk are a
known source of out-of-order folios.

## Privy trial

A private, internal examination of each presbytery member's own life and doctrine,
conducted periodically by the court itself — the venue where Mr Gilbert Power was recorded
"not approven" for years even after his reposition.

## Libel

Not defamation in the modern legal sense, but the formal, numbered written accusation
opening a discipline case — e.g. Uchtred McDowell of French's 18-article libel against Mr
Gilbert Power.

## Compurgation

Clearing oneself of an accusation by solemn oath (sometimes with oath-helpers), offered to
Alexander McCulloch of Ardwell as an alternative to producing his accuser's proof.

## Malignant

The Covenanter-era term for a person judged disaffected to the Kirk's cause — royalist
sympathisers, or (after 1648) anyone who supported the Engagement — subject to censure,
exclusion from the Covenant, and civil purge.

## The Engagement

The December 1647 secret agreement between the captive Charles I and a faction of Scots
nobles (led by the Duke of Hamilton) to invade England on his behalf in return for a
promised three-year establishment of Presbyterianism there. Its military defeat at Preston
(August 1648) triggered the purge this volume documents in detail.

## National Covenant / Solemn League and Covenant

The 1638 National Covenant bound Scotland to resist changes to Reformed church government;
the 1643 Solemn League and Covenant extended a similar bond to England and Ireland as a
condition of Scots military support in the English Civil War. "Subscribing the Covenant" —
and refusing to — is the recurring loyalty test across the 1640s folios.

## Reponed / reposition

Restored to an office or ministry after a suspension or deposition — the outcome of Mr
Gilbert Power's appeal to the General Assembly.

## Sackcloth

The standard public-penance garment/posture for a scandal such as fornication or adultery,
performed before the congregation, often at more than one parish kirk in sequence for a
serious offence.

## Teind

The Scots term for tithe — a tenth of a parish's produce owed to support the church,
frequently in dispute (as arrears) throughout the Glenluce planting saga.

## Glebe and manse

The glebe is the land, and the manse the house, allotted to a parish minister as part of
his living — recurring subjects of dispute wherever a new charge (like the new kirk of
Glenluce) is being established.

## Mortification

A permanent grant or endowment of money or land for a religious or charitable purpose — the
Glenluce Abbey mortification is the financial engine behind the parish's eventual planting.

## Heritor

A landowner within a parish, liable (in proportion to his land) for its share of stipend,
kirk repairs, and other parish charges — and, collectively, "the heritors" are the body the
presbytery negotiates with (and sometimes sues) over Glenluce's finances.

## Common head

A set theological topic assigned to a presbytery member to write and defend before the
court as part of the regular "exercise" — a recurring feature of ordinary presbytery
business between discipline cases (e.g. *de statu primi hominis*, *de libero arbitrio*).
