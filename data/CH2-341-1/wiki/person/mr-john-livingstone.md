Minister of **Stranraer** and, for most of the volume's span, the presbytery's most senior
and most frequently absent member — repeatedly pulled away to Ireland or Edinburgh on kirk
business too important to refuse, which makes his comings and goings a useful clock for
dating the surrounding folios.

## The Irish connection

Livingstone is sent to Ireland by the commissioners of the kirk more than once: in 1642
(during the height of the Power case, prompting the presbytery to try, unsuccessfully, to
keep him home) [img_0018, img_0026], again by direction of the General Assembly's
commissioners in June 1643 [img_0037–0040], and again in April 1645, "necessitated" at short
notice [img_0070]. Each absence leaves the presbytery scrambling to cover his Stranraer
pulpit and, more than once, to defend his eventual return against other congregations trying
to poach him.

## Fending off rival calls

Glasgow's town council, kirk session, and presbytery jointly petition to have him
transported to Blackfriars kirk, Glasgow — an answer the presbytery delays rather than
grants [img_113]. He is later called before the Committee of the Kirk over a possible
transportation to "the parish of [Rowtane]," and the presbytery sends **Turnbull and
Garthland** to Edinburgh specifically to oppose it [img_96]. Ancrum similarly tries to have
him transported in 1648, again unsuccessfully [img_130]. Stranraer keeps him for the whole
volume.

## Diplomat and fixer

Beyond his own pulpit, Livingstone is the presbytery's go-to negotiator: he is commissioned
to Edinburgh to secure a competent Stranraer stipend from the rents of the bishopric of
Galloway and reports success [img_124–125]; he is sent to enforce a removal decreet against
a tenant of Glenluce Abbey [img_100]; and he is charged, with Turnbull, to collect "the
remarkable passages of the late work of God in this kingdom" for the Commission of the Kirk
— a striking early instance of the Kirk systematically gathering providence-narratives
[img_88].
