# CH2/341/1 — Image 154 (folios 153v–154)

**James Ross of Balneil's answers continue, articles 3–7** (his rebuttal of the
minister's nine points, imgs 152–153). Left page (foliated **153a** = folio 153
verso), badly faded: on ★ **article 3** he denies counselling any act against the
committee, describing an exchange with **Garthland** about an oath ("if the oath were
no other than one of faithful administration, acceptance was equivalent"); on
**article 4** he flatly **denies advising Major McDougall** ("the gentleman himself
will testify the contrary"). The right page (folio 154) is clearer: on ★★ **article 5
(the protection)** he argues the shire was quartered on unequally, money was promised
to **Daniel Mowat**, and — crucially — that ★★ **his protection was given by the Earl
of Callendar, not Duke Hamilton** ("it is not likely, Callendar being the giver of
that protection, and not the Duke"); on ★ **article 6** he denies pressing others to
read the **Committee of Estates' observations** (there was a committee act ordaining
someone to read it; he professed himself "indifferent whether it be read or not"); and
on ★ **article 7 (spreading "civil novels")** he refers himself to the session, denying
all rumour-mongering **except that he may have spoken of "the prevailing of the English
army"** (continued next opening).

## Notes & context
- ★★ **"Callendar, not the Duke."** The sharpest single point in Balneil's defence: he
  concedes he **had a protection**, but insists it came from **the Earl of Callendar**
  (the Engager lieutenant-general), **not from Duke Hamilton** — an attempt to soften
  the charge by naming the lesser figure. It also confirms how routinely Galloway lairds
  obtained protections from the Engager command.
- ★★ **The equity-of-quartering argument.** Balneil turns the tables: the burden of
  quartering was laid on him unequally, contrary to Parliament's ordinance of
  **proportional relief**; if others were freed by their protections, he ought to be too.
  A revealing window on how **free quarter** was distributed and resented in 1648
  Wigtownshire, and on the role of **Daniel Mowat** as a money-broker.
- ★ **"Indifferent whether it be read or not."** On the Estates' anti-Kirk observations,
  Balneil's defence is studied neutrality — he denies pressing the reading and claims he
  said he was indifferent. Illustrates how dangerous mere *association* with Engager
  propaganda had become.
- ★ **The one admitted rumour: "the prevailing of the English army."** Balneil concedes
  he may have spoken of the **success of the English (Parliamentarian/Cromwellian) army**
  — a datable trace of Galloway news-talk in late 1648, and the one "civil novel" he owns.
- Read with imgs 152–153, this completes the Balneil dossier through article 7; article 7
  and any remaining articles continue on img 155.
