# CH2/341/1 — Image 38 (folios 37v–38), 26 July 1643 — Blair's supplication ends; Blair & French submit to the presbytery

Left page (folio **37a**, faded) continues **Mr James Blair's supplication**: French, by
"narrative and insinuations," has done his utmost "**to take away my good name**" and
make Blair's ministry "unprofitable" and his name "infamous"; Blair asks the presbytery
to weigh French's malicious expressions and to clear him of the claim that he was a
**partisan ("traitor") for Power at a meeting** — which Blair says he attended "**only
accidentally**," alongside the **lairds of Logan and Ellaster**, speaking for neither
side. Right page (folio **38**): Blair denies acting as go-between for **Airds, the
Sheriff, or Garthland** against French, and denies the **"letters of exhibition"** French
alleges; subscribed **Mr James Blair**. The presbytery then has **both Blair and French
submit their whole differences to it** — and its **declaration/judgment follows**.

### Modern English (partial — left page faded)
[Blair, cont.:] …in his complaint and accusation, it is obvious that, "**by narrative and
insinuations, he has done his utmost in his power to assail my good name**" and dispossess
his hearers' hearts, with "things sorely unbeseeming a Christian, much less a minister" —
plainly aiming "**to make my ministry unprofitable and my name infamous**." The matters
in French's complaint being "no ways answerable to the frame of his narrative," such
"dangerous preparatives" would make ministers contemptible. Blair therefore entreats the
brethren to weigh French's expressions — (1) "most unconscionable"; (2) "in a malicious
manner"; (3) that French "was disappointed"; and lastly, that by unjust dealing he was
driven to hinder the parties — expressions that not only serve the malicious to
**traduce** Blair, but prejudice the better-affected; the more so **since it remains in
the records**, which, unless deleted or otherwise cleared, cannot satisfy or repair his
prejudice. He craves the presbytery address French's defamation, **where French affirms
Blair was a "traitor" (partisan) for Power** — whereas Blair "**came only accidentally,
was not called to that meeting** by French, and [came] with the lairds of Logan and
Ellaster," and "**neither spoke nor did anything for French nor for Power.**"

### Modern English
[Blair, concl.:] …confident these gentlemen will not deny it. As for the matter he says
Blair "abstracted from him," Blair **never withheld anything of French's for a moment in
a scandalous way**; as soon as he could find it, he produced it, and meanwhile was
willing to make it good; neither did the presbytery nor Power recant, but were willing to
**stand to the agreement**. Where French affirms that Blair **acted as go-between (had
"intromission") for my lord of Airds, the Sheriff, or Garthland** — Blair declares that
**my lord of Airds did once speak to him about the desire of the Lady French**, and Blair
"gave him his contentment," but **neither the Sheriff nor Garthland ever spoke to him**
about it. And where French claims Blair **rode to Edinburgh and brought home "letters of
exhibition"** delivered to a messenger to charge French — **Blair "never heard of such
letters till this complaint."** To prove French "cared not for the production but only to
use the want of it to scandalize" Blair's name, French should remember the answer he gave
when Blair delivered the same to him. **Whenever French can verify his accusation and
produce his letters of exhibition** (dated before delivery), and name the messenger, Blair
will **satisfy the judicatory and pay French's charges of raising those letters**. He
commends all to the brethren's "grave and wise consideration," craving their answer.
**Subscribed: Mr James Blair.**

The presbytery craved that **Mr James Blair and the laird of French submit their whole
differences to it**; **that day, both did submit.** The presbytery's **declaration of its
judgment of the differences between the laird of French and Mr James Blair follows** [→].

## Notes & context
- **The presbytery turns arbiter.** Rather than try Blair's defamation complaint
  adversarially, the court has **both parties formally submit their whole differences to
  it** (a *submission to arbitration*), then gives a **declaration/judgment** (next
  opening). A different, conciliatory mode from the Power prosecution — worth contrasting
  in the wiki.
- **The gentry network around French, named.** Blair's defence lists the **laird of
  French's alleged agents/allies** — **my lord of Airds, the Sheriff (of Galloway
  younger = Agnew of Lochnaw), the laird of Garthland** — and the **lairds of Logan and
  Ellaster** at the disputed meeting. This maps the **local gentry faction** behind the
  church-court fight (French's side), a key deliverable for the "events/locations/people"
  cross-linking. Note **the Lady French** (French's wife/mother), whose "desire" Airds
  conveyed to Blair.
- **"Letters of exhibition."** A recurring point: French allegedly had Blair **ride to
  Edinburgh for "letters of exhibition"** (a legal writ compelling production of
  documents) — which Blair denies. Glossary candidate; and a window into how these
  disputes reached the central courts in Edinburgh.
- **The record as a weapon.** Blair stresses the defamation "**remains in your records**"
  and cannot be undone "unless deleted or otherwise cleared" — again showing the parties'
  acute awareness that **what the register preserves matters** (cf. img_0037's suppressed
  supplication; img_0029's back-fill).
- **Palaeography / language applied:** `intromet/intromission` = to deal with / handle
  (esp. another's affairs or goods); `abstracted` = withheld/kept back; `letters of
  exhibition` = a writ ordering production of documents; `traduce` = defame; `traitor
  for my parson` here = *partisan* (one who takes a party's side), not treason; `assay` =
  assail/attempt; `soris unbeseeming` = sorely unbecoming. **Left page ink browned** —
  several readings conjectural. Capital **W** for *w/wh*. Dates 1643 as written.
