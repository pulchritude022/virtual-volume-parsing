Minister of [Stoneykirk](entity:place/stoneykirk) through the 1630s–40s, and the subject of
the volume's single longest-running case: see
[Mr Gilbert Power's deposition and reposition](entity:event/mr-gilbert-power-s-deposition-and-reposition)
for the full narrative. This page collects what the record shows of the man himself, apart
from the mechanics of the case against him.

## Before the case

Power appears in the earliest folios as a moneylender of sorts — a "Mr Gilbert" collecting
annualrent (interest) in cash and in kind (meal and bere from **David McCulloch**) — a
detail that reads differently once you know the eventual usury charge against him
[img_0002]. He had, by his accusers' account, also written testaments for parishioners,
including the disputed will of the late **Peter McCulloch** that became a central plank of
the libel against him [img_0032–0033].

## Under accusation

Power's answers to the 18-article libel are combative rather than contrite: he reframes the
Tarbret payment as an innocent broker's fee, defends his annualrent as lawful, and mounts a
sustained procedural fight — objecting that his accusers were effectively sitting as judges,
that he was denied cross-examination and a process extract, and (most pointedly) alleging
that **Turnbull, Livingstone, and George Dick** had themselves taken bribes ("buds") from
Ardwell and Killaster — a charge Turnbull flatly denied [img_0024, img_0034–0036].

## Deposed, and a parish split down the middle

When the presbytery deposed him in 1642 it did so "all in one voice, none excepted" — but
the parish itself was not so unanimous. **George McCulloch and six other parishioners**
petitioned for his restoration even as Stoneykirk's own kirk session had petitioned against
him, describing itself as "a desolate people" whose comfort his ministry no longer gave
[img_0017, img_0043 sqq.]. Even his own daughter, **Marion Power**, appears in the record
disturbing a service against Mr Alexander Turnbull — a glimpse of how personally the
dispute had come to divide the parish [img_0043].

## Vindicated, then still distrusted

The General Assembly's reversal of his deposition (see the event page) should have settled
the matter, but for nearly two years the Synod of Galloway and several named presbytery
members quietly declined to treat him as "approved" at successive privy trials even while
listing him as a member — a passive-aggressive resistance to a national ruling its
protagonists (George Dick, John McClellan, Hew Henderson, Uchtred McDowell of French)
evidently disliked. Only in August 1644 did Power force the Assembly's act into the
presbytery's own book.

## Old age and reconciliation

By his last years in the record Power is "old and infirm," and asks only pragmatic things of
the men who once deposed him: pay his arrears, let him keep his stipend till a colleague
arrives, secure his provision after death, let him "dwell out his years unmolested." He
offers to fund most of a helper's salary himself out of his own stipend and the glebe of
Kirkmadrine. The heritors agree point by point — a genuine, if wary, reconciliation
[img_0066–0067].
