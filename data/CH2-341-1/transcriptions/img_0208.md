### Modern English
… by reason of the troubles of the time he would do no further in the matter [of the manse]. But as for the scandal lying on **Gilbert Kennedy of Aviolen** within this parish, the presbytery renews the former ordinance. **Mr John Park reports that the Session of Stranraer had taken trial and enquired at Jonet Semple what she would give in the matter of the scandal lying on the said Gilbert Kennedy; and that she declared as follows:**
> **1.** That she, herself being servant to the said Gilbert Kennedy, knew that one **Margaret Wilson** (the servant in old **Andrew Cowan's** house) followed her master to Edinburgh; and that the said Gilbert gave out to his own wife and others that the said Margaret was with child to a serving man of the town named **James Williamson** — and that the said James Williamson, then living in Edinburgh in service and (as was prejudicial) then liable to die, **declared that if the woman was with child, it was not to him, but to her master.**
> **2.** That the said Gilbert said that [—] John, her servant, rode a little before him with his horse, and that he saw them in an interchange of words anent Jonet McMillan, parishioner of Inch; and that upon the bill of clamour raised within this parish.

## Notes & context
★★★ **The Gilbert Kennedy adultery scandal — a full session deposition (with witness-tampering).** The duellist **Gilbert Kennedy of Aviolen** (imgs 185–205) is now consumed by a **detailed adultery case**, deposed in four numbered points by his own servant **Jonet Semple**: that **Margaret Wilson** (a servant in "old Andrew Cowan's house") "followed her master [Kennedy] to Edinburgh"; that Kennedy tried to pin her pregnancy on a serving man, **James Williamson**, who on his deathbed swore **"if the woman was with child, it was not to him, but to her master"**; that Kennedy also lay with **Jonet Marowin** in Inch and was entangled with **Jonet McMillan**; and — most damning for the process — that **Andrew Marbie carried a message from Kennedy to a witness ("the said John"), asking him to retract his testimony in exchange for reward.** Marbie admits saying so but claims he "had no commission from Gilbert Kennedy." This is an unusually vivid, novelistic kirk-session sexual/witness-tampering record; the cluster of named servants and women is rich prosopography.

★★ **"Served about Dublin in 1646 … under the Parliament of England."** A finely calibrated malignancy case: **Wilfred Campbell of Kirkcolm** clears himself for the Covenant by producing a paper and swearing that his soldiering was **"about Dublin in 1646, as a captain of foot, under the command of the Parliament of England,"** and that he had not since served "in any malignant or rebellious army." Service under the *English Parliament* in Ireland (against the Irish confederates) is treated as purgeable — unlike service in Munro's or the royalist forces — a nice index of the Kirk's fine distinctions among ex-soldiers. (Cf. the Donaghadee testimonial for Robert Campbell, img 207; the Ulster–Rhins traffic runs through both.)

★ **The manse stalled by the war.** Mr John Dick notes that **"by reason of the troubles of the time"** he would do no further with the manse for now (cf. img 206) — the post-Dunbar disruption reaching even the building works. Routine: **Arisemman still not recovered**; the **witches' residence** cannot be traced; the **widows-and-orphans money** is booked in the session records.
