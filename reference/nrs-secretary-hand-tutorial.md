# NRS "Scottish Handwriting" — 1-Hour Basic Tutorial (captured notes)

Faithful notes captured from the National Records of Scotland free tutorial at
<https://www.scotlandspeople.gov.uk/scottish-handwriting/tutorials/1-hour-basic-tutorial>
(steps 1–6), for **private research use** priming AI transcription of this project's
records. © National Records of Scotland — see their site for the original pages and the
letter-form images this text refers to. The distilled, volume-specific working guide is
[scottish-secretary-hand.md](scottish-secretary-hand.md); this file preserves the
tutorial's own structure and emphasis.

The tutorial teaches **Secretary Hand**, the administrative/business script used across
western Europe c. 1500–1750 and dominant in Scottish records of that period.

## Step 1 — What Secretary Hand looks like (two examples)
- **1564 testament:** most letters are in Secretary Hand forms — note especially
  `a b c d e g h k r s t`. Alongside the hand, expect these recurring difficulties:
  **Latin numerals; archaic letters (yogh ȝ and thorn þ); Scots vernacular words;
  phonetic spelling; abbreviations; interchangeable letters** (`u/v/w` variants; `i/j`
  variants).
- **1722 testament:** later hands are messier — a mix of untidy Secretary Hand, Italic,
  other styles, and idiosyncratic forms of the writer's own invention.
- Tutorial's advice: don't labour over the sample images; learn the letters first.

## Step 2 — The two key letters: `e` and `s`
- **`e`** looks like the Greek letter **theta (θ)** — a loop with a bar. It is the most
  common vowel, so recognising it instantly is the single biggest speed gain.
- **`s`** has two forms: the **long `s`** (descends; f-like) used at the **start/middle**
  of words, and the **short/round `s`** used at word **ends**. The word *scandalous*
  shows both (long-s front, round-s near the end).

## Step 3 — Other problem letters: `a b c d h r t`
- `a` — like a modern *handwritten* `a`, with a diagonal "attacking stroke" bottom-left→top-right (e.g. *thame*).
- `b` — like a modern **capital** `B` (e.g. *box*, *breithren*).
- `c` — two sides of a square / like a modern `r`; vertical often curved. **Confuse with `t` and `r`.**
- `d` — near-modern but **dangerously like Secretary `e`** — check by context.
- `h` — like a **butcher's hook**, descends below the line; sloppy at word-ends (e.g. *hundreth*, *schilling*).
- `r` — two forms: **curly `r`** and **descending `r`**.
- `t` — very like `c` (vertical + horizontal stroke; top of vertical curls right).

## Step 4 — Spelling conventions and Scots
- Standard spelling is an **18th-century invention**; before ~1850 spelling is
  inconsistent, especially for **personal and place names**.
- Words are often **phonetic**: *signe* (not "figure" — watch the long-s), *contayneing*
  (= containing). Playful closing examples: *improove, abilitie, rede, handrittin,
  foaneticallie*.
- **Rule:** intuition is a starting point only — always check the word letter-by-letter
  and confirm the spelling. A word is either a familiar word spelled oddly, or an
  unfamiliar word that only looks familiar.

## Step 5 — "Sic braw secretarie hand" (practical application)
- Problem lowercase letters to drill: `a b c d e h r s t w`.
- Practise writing the Scots phrase **"sic braw secretarie hand"** ("such fine secretary
  hand") — don't worry about neatness (real documents are far messier).
- Reading old handwriting is like golf: **mental rehearsal and set expectations** (Scots
  vocabulary, phonetic spelling, the letterforms) make you read faster and more accurately.

## Step 6 — Self-test
- Identify these letters from samples (the tutorial's test set): **c, h, b, s, d, e,
  s (alternate form), a, r, t** — i.e. exactly the confusable set drilled above.
