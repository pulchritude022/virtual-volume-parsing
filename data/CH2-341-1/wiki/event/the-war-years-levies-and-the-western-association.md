Through the 1640s the presbytery was not only a spiritual court but a cog in the war effort of
the Covenanting state. The register records the church's part in **raising men and money** for
successive campaigns — against Montrose, for the Engagement (and then against its compliers),
and finally against the invading English — routed in the south-west through the
**Western Association** of the shires.

## Mobilisation and the pulpit

When the state needed soldiers, it worked partly through the kirk. The presbytery deals with
**levies, musters, and rendezvous**, with the **quartering** of troops, and with the reading
of state proclamations from the pulpit. Ministers are appointed as **army chaplains** and
"regimental chaplains," and the court corresponds with the **Committee of War** and the
**Committee of Estates** over the shire's contribution.

## The Western Association

Much of this ran through the **Western Association** — the covenanting combination of Ayr,
Wigtown, Kirkcudbright and their presbyteries for the defence of the west. Its business of
manpower and money appears repeatedly as an item on the presbytery's agenda, tying this small
court into a regional war-machine.

## Purging the army

The same years bring the reverse duty: **purging the army and the judicatories of malignants**
after the Engagement's defeat — see the
[post-Engagement purge](entity:event/the-post-engagement-purge-of-malignants). The presbytery
must certify who is fit to serve, refusing "malignants" even as it presses the loyal to turn
out.

## Context

Read alongside the [timeline](timeline.html), these entries show a Galloway court keeping step
with every turn of the national war — a reminder that the kirk session and the muster-roll were
two faces of the same Covenanted state.
