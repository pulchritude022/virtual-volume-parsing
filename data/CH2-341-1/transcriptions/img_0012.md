# CH2/341/1 — Image 12 (folios 11v–12), Nov–Dec 1642 — Power suspended

Left page (folio **11a**) is the **turning point**: after admonishing (but
retaining) Mr James Blair, and after witness attendance, the presbytery reads
French's motion and — on the point of **cloaking Robert Tarbret's adultery and
taking a bribe** — **suspends Mr Gilbert Power from his ministry** until the
process is finally decided ("agreed by all but two"). Right page (folio **12**)
denies Power a copy of the depositions, arranges **pulpit supply for the vacant
Stoneykirk**, and opens a new sederunt — **Stranraer, Wednesday 15 December
1642** — with more witness examination.

### Modern English
[French's Blair-motion concludes: only when compelled did Blair find and exhibit
the minute; wherefore French asks the presbytery to consider Blair's carriage and
remove him. *Subscribed: French younger.*] The presbytery, considering the first
reason, thinks **Mr James Blair's carriage** in this matter has sometimes been
"rather like a pleader than an indifferent person," and thought good that he be
**admonished** — which was done; they ordain him to keep his seat for now, unless
they perceive more of such behaviour. Blair compeared, was admonished, acknowledged
he reverenced the presbytery's judgment, and is continued to sit.

**George Jamison** and **William Campbell**, witnesses summoned by the Kirkmaiden
kirk officer, were called: Campbell did not compear; Jamison compeared, but French
"passed from" (declined to use) him.

**French then gives in a paper:** since the process cannot be fully concluded this
day, and since there is already "as much proven as would depose a minister," he
craves that the presbytery **suspend** Power until the process is ended, and
meanwhile have a care of "that poor destitute congregation." *Subscribed: French
younger.* **The presbytery, having read the paper — and in consideration of the
article on cloaking Robert Tarbret's adultery and taking a bribe for it, with the
witness depositions — finds it fitting that Mr Gilbert be removed from his ministry
until the final deciding of the process, and hereby suspends him**, without
prejudice to further consideration or censure on this and the rest of the libel.
**This is agreed to by all but two.**

### Modern English
Power asked for a copy of the witnesses' depositions; the presbytery thinks it
"informal and needless" to give them (they should be kept close until the advising
of the process), but he may have the other process papers. The **Ruling Elder of
Stoneykirk** asked that a minister be appointed to preach there next Sabbath; the
presbytery **ordains Mr John Livingston to preach at Stoneykirk** (supplying the
suspended Power's pulpit). Mr George Dick and Mr John Dick are excused for urgent
affairs; the next meeting is this day month (Porterfield to make the exercise,
Blair to add, Hutcheson to give in his theses).

**Presbytery at Stranraer, Wednesday 15 December 1642.** Porterfield made and Blair
added, in course, on Psalm 19; approved, only Porterfield admonished for long
sermons. Absent: Mr George Dick, Mr John Dick, Mr Alexander Turnbull, and the
Ruling Elders of Glenluce and Inch. **Witnesses called** (compeared): **[John]
Agnew, younger of Lochnaw**, Alexander McDowell, John Wilson, Jean Hutcheson,
[Ellen] McIlSock[?], Effrik Bafoir[?]. **Mr Gilbert admits** Alexander McDowell and
John Wilson; **objects against the Sheriff younger** (French's brother-in-law and
near cousin), who declines to testify since he is excepted against in that kind;
and **objects against Jean Hutcheson and the other two women**, as being "common"
and ignorant.

## Notes & context
- **The headline event: suspension.** After weeks of libel, answers, and
  supplications (imgs 0004–0011), the presbytery **suspends Mr Gilbert Power from
  the ministry of Stoneykirk**, pending the final decision — specifically on the
  charge of **cloaking Robert Tarbret's adultery and taking a bribe**, backed by
  the witness depositions. Only two members dissented. The kirk's care for "that
  poor destitute congregation" prompts immediate **pulpit supply by Mr John
  Livingston**.
- **The kinship map sharpens.** Power objects to "**the Sheriff younger**" as
  French's **brother-in-law and near cousin** — and a new witness, **[John] Agnew,
  younger of Lochnaw**, ties in the **Agnews of Lochnaw**, hereditary Sheriffs of
  Wigtown. This locates French within the county's ruling kin-network (Sheriff,
  Lochnaw) and explains the "old Sheriff / Sheriff elder / Sheriff younger"
  references running through imgs 0007–0009.
- **Witness-objection procedure.** Power tries to exclude witnesses as *interested*
  (the Sheriff younger — kin) or *incompetent* ("common" and ignorant women — Jean
  Hutcheson et al.), a standard defence tactic; he *admits* Alexander McDowell and
  John Wilson.
- **New people:** **George Jamison**, **William Campbell** (summoned witnesses),
  **[John] Agnew of Lochnaw**, **Alexander McDowell**, **John Wilson**, **Jean
  Hutcheson**, **[Ellen] McIlSock[?]**, **Effrik Bafoir[?]**, and the **Sheriff
  younger**.
- **Palaeography applied:** `pass from his ministrie` = be removed/suspended;
  `excepted against` = formally objected to (as a witness/judge); `common` (of a
  witness) = of common/low standing or ill repute; `made`/`added`/`in course` =
  roles in the presbytery's exercise (set expository sermon); `theses` = disputation
  points; `Ps. 19` = Psalm 19. `y`+superscript → *th-*.
