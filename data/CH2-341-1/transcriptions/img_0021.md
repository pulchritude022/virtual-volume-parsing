# CH2/341/1 — Image 21 (folios 20v–21), 6 April 1643

Left page (folio **20a**) narrows French's witnesses to four named men, arranges to
write to the Wigtown witnesses, and — at the **6 April 1643** sederunt —
**administers the £2000 Irish-relief fund** in detailed merk-allocations to each
parish with named lay distributors. Right page (folio **21**) is a **witness
commission**: William McDowell (whom Power objects to) is deposed by four
commissioners on the **perjury** charge — Power's swearing that poinded goods (wine
at the Wigtown cross) were "his own proper goods."

### Modern English
[The distributors, on] a note of their former disbursements, are then — upon the
testificate — to advance more till the next presbytery day. French reports that
**William McDowell**, one of the three Wigtown witnesses, has come, but the other
two — **John McDowell (his natural brother)** and **Patrick Cabra, messenger** — have
not. **Power objects to William McDowell** as "an ordinary swearer, under process of
the kirk, who makes no conscience of his oath." The presbytery — since McDowell is
far distant and may not return, and the exception is alleged but unproven — orders
**four commissioners (Mr James Blair, Mr John Livingston, the laird of Garthland,
Fergus Kennedy)** to take his oath and deposition on the interrogatories; if next day
Power proves his exception (or any relevant one), the deposition is to be suppressed,
otherwise used. *Mr John Livingston, Clerk.* Power, called to hear the oath,
**protested against it**. William McDowell, questioned whether anyone had dealt with
him to say anything but truth, answers no; questioned about the oath Power swore at
**Wigtown**, he deposes: **James French brought wine, openly, to the cross; Mr
Gilbert came and swore the goods to be his own proper goods without reservation; the
messenger delivered the goods to Mr Gilbert, who delivered them back [to French].**

## Notes & context
- **The perjury charge, instanced.** William McDowell's deposition gives the
  concrete "perjury": at the **Wigtown cross**, wine (goods) had been brought for
  poinding/sale; **Power swore they were "his own proper goods" without
  reservation**, so the messenger released them to him and he returned them — i.e.
  Power gave a false oath to spring another man's goods from legal diligence. This is
  the kind of "criminal" article Power wanted remitted to the civil court.
- **Careful witness procedure.** Power's stock tactic — impeaching a witness as an
  **"ordinary swearer" under kirk censure** — is met by a **conditional commission**:
  take the deposition now (the man is "far distant"), but suppress it if the
  objection is later proven. The commissioners are heavyweight: **Blair, Livingston,
  the laird of Garthland, Fergus Kennedy**.
- **Irish relief, fully itemised.** Folio 20a is a rare, complete **relief-disbursement
  schedule**: £2000 total, 500 merks reserved at Stranraer, and 50–200 merks to each
  parish with **named lay distributors** — a trove of ordinary parishioners' names
  (**Ninian & Thomas Boyd**, **James Gordon**, **Wm Kennedy**, **Wm Pirie younger**,
  and a **McCulloch**) for the people index and genealogy.
- **New people:** **William McDowell** & **John McDowell** (brothers, witnesses),
  **Patrick Cabra** (messenger), **Fergus Kennedy** (commissioner), **James French**
  (wine at the cross), and the relief distributors above.
- **Palaeography applied:** `ane ordinary swearer` = a habitual profane swearer;
  `makes not conscience of his oath` = has no scruple about swearing falsely;
  `poinding` context — goods "brought to the cross" for public sale/arrest;
  `condescends to leade` = undertakes to bring/examine (witnesses); `testificat` =
  a certificate of need. Dates "1642" Old Style. `y`+superscript → *th-*.
