# CH2/341/1 — Image 153 (folios 152v–153)

The **case of James Ross of Balneil in full**: the minister's **nine articles of
malignancy** (left page, foliated **152a** = folio 152 verso) and **Balneil's
point-by-point answer** (right page, folio 153). The charges: (1) furthering the
unlawful Engagement; (2) **refusing to subscribe the anti-Engagement supplication** to
Parliament; (3) counselling an act to be taken of the committee men for raising the
Engagement, and holding that the Earl of Galloway should [name?] his own officers; (4)
advising **Major McDougall**; (5) ★ **taking a protection [from Duke Hamilton] and, in
face of the committee, refusing to pay his proportions** "because he had a protection";
(6) pressing others to read the **Committee of Estates' observations against the
Assembly's declarations**; (7) being "a spreader of civil novels [rumours]"; (8) being
**disaffected to the work of God and conversing with malignants**; (9) [a servant/miner
pressed to be a soldier who fled]. Balneil answers that ★★ **he cannot be justly
charged with malignancy** — humbly acknowledging his breaches of the Covenant and that
he did not reform his life as he ought — and rebuts the first articles in detail
(the Michaelmas commission predated the Engagement; he never saw the supplication and
was merely absent, as he told **Mr John Crosse**; etc.), craving to be **not esteemed
malignant nor delayed from taking the Covenant**.

*(Balneil's answers, article by article.)*

...satisfaction, as you shall think expedient; and humbly craves that, for his more

**(1)** *Charged with taking a commission to Parliament at Michaelmas:* he answers there
can be **no malignancy therein, since at the time of taking it the Engagement was not
yet passed, nor known to any**; the friends were necessitated to choose a commissioner
to Parliament so the questions might be the more readily decided... he would meddle no
more than the rest, nor had any other design.

necessary affairs, **as he signified to Mr John Crosse**; and to the declarations he
refers himself.

## Notes & context
- ★★ **The complete Balneil charge-and-answer.** This is the fullest single dossier of
  the whole purge in these openings: a heritor's **nine-article indictment for
  malignancy** and his careful, lawyerly **rebuttal**. It is prime person-page material
  for **James Ross of Balneil**, and a model of how a Galloway laird defended himself —
  conceding general "breaches of the Covenant" while denying each specific political
  charge.
- ★ **The protection-from-Duke-Hamilton charge (art. 5).** The same "protection" motif
  that recurs across imgs 146–152 is here a formal article: refusing to pay his
  "proportions" to the committee *because he held a protection*. A crisp definition of
  what counted as malignancy on the ground.
- ★ **"Civil novels" and reading the Estates' observations (arts. 6–7).** The charges
  reach beyond deeds to **words and reading matter** — pressing others to read the
  Engager Committee of Estates' answers against the Kirk's declarations, and "spreading
  civil novels [rumours]." Evidence of an information war at parish level.
- ★ **Balneil's defence names Mr John Crosse** as a witness to his absence from the
  supplication-signing — a small cross-link to the presbytery clerk/brother.
- The **Michaelmas-commission defence** (art. 1) is chronologically pointed: Balneil
  argues the commission predated the Engagement's passage, so cannot be malignant — a
  precise dating argument worth preserving.
