# CH2/341/1 — Image 75 (folios 74v–75), June–July 1645 — "turning the riddle": a sieve-divination case; disorder at Stoneykirk

Left page (folio **74a**): **Hutcheson and Bell not excused for their absence at Kirkcudbright**; then
a **striking superstition case** — a person **summoned for "turning the riddle" (sieve divination) to
discover who had stolen certain clothes out of [Garthland]**: they confessed that **[—] Ross (in
Stranraer) and [—] Gilmore turned the riddle**, and, blessing it, said **"By St Peter and St Paul,
such a body stole them" / "such a body stole them not," naming each householder in turn** to identify
the thief; the presbytery **ordains the said [Edilston?] to repair to the kirk of Stoneykirk to
undergo censure according to the quality of the fault**, and **not to remove till satisfaction given.**
Then further discipline: **Jonet Corhead and Jon McDowell** enjoined not to remove till they satisfy;
**Jon Stuart and [J.] Nobell** summoned for scandal; and one summoned for **slandering the session of
Stoneykirk** (alleging it "takes no order with profaners"). Right page (folio **75**, very faded):
**Mr Gilbert Power reports that he and the session of Stoneykirk have been trying [to order the
parish], affirming there is much disorder there, but could get no trial of it**; then the **9 July
1645 sederunt** — **Mr George Dick handles the common head ("de libero arbitrio") and is approved**
(after the storm delay, img 0074); **absent Turnbull**; a compearance touching **Bell and Glenluce.**

## Notes & context
- **★ "Turning the riddle" — sieve-and-shears divination in Galloway (1645).** The volume's first
  clear **popular-magic / superstition case**: to find out **who had stolen clothes from [Garthland]**,
  parishioners **"turned the riddle"** — the well-attested *coscinomancy* rite in which a sieve
  (riddle) is balanced on shears and made to turn while a formula is spoken over each suspect: here
  **"By St Peter and St Paul, such a body stole them / stole them not,"** running through the
  householders until the sieve "answers." The kirk treated this as **charming/superstition** (not
  felony witchcraft, but a scandalous "fault"), and sent the offender to **public censure at
  Stoneykirk.** A vivid, quotable window into folk belief and how the presbytery policed it — an
  excellent glossary/theme node (**"turning the riddle" / sieve divination**) and a set of named
  parishioners (**[—] Ross, [—] Gilmore, [—] Edilston, Jonet Corhead, Jon McDowell**). Handle the
  names with care (very faded).
- **Disorder at Stoneykirk that resists investigation.** **Power reports "much disorder in the
  parish" at the Stoneykirk visitation but "could get no trial of it"** — the aged minister's parish
  slipping his grip (cf. the ruinous kirk and unpaid stipend, img 0072), reinforcing why a colleague
  was so needed. Note for his person-page and the Stoneykirk parish page.
- **The free-will common head finally delivered.** After the **storm stopped it on 25 June** (img
  0074), **George Dick delivers the common head "de libero arbitrio" (on free will) on 9 July 1645
  and is approved** — closing that little thread.
- **Absence enforced.** **Hutcheson and Bell "not excused" for missing [a meeting/visitation at]
  Kirkcudbright** — the presbytery holding its members to attendance across the region.
- **Palaeography / language applied:** `turning the riddle` = sieve-and-shears divination
  (coscinomancy) to identify a thief; `Be St Peter and St Paul… such a body stole them` = the
  divinatory formula spoken over each suspect; `undergo the publick censure according to the qualitie
  of the fault` = do public penance proportioned to the offence; `could get no tryall of it` = could
  obtain no proof; `common head "de libero arbitrio"` = the set disputation on free will. **Folio 75
  is very badly washed** — large stretches illegible and provisional. Capital **W** for *w/wh*.
  Dates 1645.
