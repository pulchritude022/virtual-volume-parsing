# CH2/341/1 — Image 26 (folios 25v–26), April 1643

Left page (folio **25a**) finishes **James Gordon's accusation** — the same
debt/horning narrative against the laird of French as Ellaster's paper — subscribed
"James Gordne." Right page (folio **26**): Gordon is **"in Culgroat"**; the presbytery
**defers** both Power's and Gordon's papers and writes to Wigtown for the books; then
a new sederunt (**18 April 1643**) records **Mr John Livingston absent, called to
Ireland with General Major Monro** (reversing img_0018), a refugee child's baptism,
and Gordon's accusation formally received.

### Modern English (paraphrase)
[Gordon's charges cont.:] since 1642 Power **preached twice in his own close**; **(6)**
he **received a conform minister who fled Ireland for fear of the rebels and tendered
him the covenant** after his preaching — more than any minister, let alone a suspended
one, might do. Gordon also "remonstrates" Power's **inhumane carriage to the laird of
French**: at the Stoneykirk visitation, before the whole presbytery, French offered to
pay in money what was justly owing of his yearly duty, but Power refused and, out of
malice, **put his bond to the register and charged him**; and, despite the suspension,
**denounced French at the horn** — again at Martinmas (refusing the offered term's
duty), and a **third time** despite an 80-merk bond of suspension — forcing French to
Edinburgh, one of the charge-days falling on the Lord's day. *Subscribed: James
Gordne.*  (This narrative duplicates Ellaster's paper, img_0019–0020.)

### Modern English (paraphrase — low confidence)
[Concluding Gordon's paper:] after the horning-registration, French complained to the
Provincial Assembly, which admonished Power not to repeat it. **These are the crimes**
of **James Gordon in Culgroat's** accusation, entreating the presbytery — "as ye tender
your honour" — to consider the **desolate estate of that poor congregation**, put the
accusations to **speedy trial**, and send someone to supply the pulpit on Sabbaths.
*Subscribed: James Gordne.* The day being far spent, the presbytery, having read
Power's and Gordon's papers, **defers both till next day** (Monday come eight days),
and orders the clerk to write to **Wigtown presbytery for the Synod/presbytery books**
and bring the two witnesses.

**Presbytery at Stranraer, 18 April 1643.** Absent: **Mr John Livingston, being called
by the Council and presbytery to go over to Ireland with General Major Monro**; Mr John
Dick appointed clerk for the day. The presbytery agreed that **a child of Mr Robert[?]
Junior** (himself absent), the mother having made confession and given signs of
repentance, should — after "the Irish oath and other things of that nature" — be
baptised by any of the brethren. At Stranraer, the same day, **James Gordon in
Culgroat's accusation against Mr Gilbert Power was received**, but its discussing [was
deferred]…

## Notes & context
- **Three accusers, one script.** James Gordon's paper repeats Andrew McDowell of
  Ellaster's almost verbatim (the conform-minister charge; the French debt/horning
  narrative to the letter). Together with Uchtred McDowell of French's original libel,
  this confirms a **coordinated prosecution** — likely one hand drafting the "post-
  suspension" supplements. Gordon is now placed: **James Gordon in Culgroat** (a
  ferm-toun near Portpatrick).
- **Livingston goes to Ireland after all.** On 25 March (img_0018) the presbytery had
  **released** Livingston from going with Monro; by **18 April** he is **absent, called
  by the Council and presbytery to go over to Ireland with General Major Monro** — a
  reversal that fits the historical John Livingston's 1643 Irish commission. A key
  datapoint for his biography.
- **A refugee baptism.** The presbytery arranges baptism for the child of an absent
  minister ("Mr Robert[?] Junior"), the mother having repented and taken "**the Irish
  oath**" — a glimpse of how the kirk received Ulster-war refugees and regularised
  their sacraments.
- **Procedure:** both Power's appellation and Gordon's accusation are held over; the
  clerk is to fetch the **Synod/presbytery books from Wigtown** and the two witnesses —
  the case is being prepared for the Synod.
- **Palaeography applied:** inner (bound) margin of the right page is largely lost →
  bracketed reconstructions (low confidence); `at the horne` = outlawed for debt;
  `regrat` = distressed/pursued; `the Irish oath` = an oath/abjuration required of
  incoming Irish; `let be` = let alone. Dates "1642" Old Style. `y`+superscript → *th-*.
