"The Engagement" was the 1647–48 pact between the captive Charles I and a faction of Scots
nobles (led by the Duke of Hamilton) to invade England on his behalf in exchange for a
promise to establish Presbyterianism there for three years — a deal the strict Kirk party
in the General Assembly regarded as an unlawful compact with a still-unreformed king. Its
military defeat at Preston (August 1648) triggered a wave of local purges across Scotland,
and the Stranraer presbytery's register captures the Rhins of Galloway's own version of it
in granular, name-by-name detail.

## Loyalty tests and a named roll of Engagers

Once the printed General Assembly declaration against the Engagement reaches the presbytery
[img_140], the machinery turns inward: every minister is tried for firmness against it (all
found sound), and the civil Committee at Stranraer is pressed to **purge its own
judicatories of malignants** [img_144–145]. A **named register of 1648 Engagers** is laid
before the committee — the laird of **Ardwell**, **Gordon of Earlston**, **MacDowall of
Garthland**, the **Earl of Galloway**, **James Ronald(s)**, **Gordon of Craiglaw**, **Thomas
Stewart** — each accused of some concrete act of compliance: buying an officer's commission,
taking a protection from Duke Hamilton or the Earl of Callendar, hiring men, or withholding
poor-relief money entrusted to them [img_146–147].

## The gentry bound at Glenluce

A recurring detail across several later depositions is that the "gentlemen of the shire
were bound at Glenluce, by a motion of [the Marquess of] Argyll's" — a concrete
subscription event, at which men including James Ross of Balneil signed the National
Covenant under direct pressure from the Kirk party's most powerful noble patron [img_161].
It is the single clearest piece of evidence in the volume for how the purge was actually
enforced on the ground.

## The Covenant-renewal drive (1649)

Into early 1649 the purge becomes constructive as well as punitive: the Commission of the
Kirk sends materials for **renewing the Covenant**, and every session is charged to search
out and report Engagement compliers so they can be **debarred** from taking it until they
satisfy the Kirk [img_150–151, img_162–164]. Sessions examine their own heritors
parish-by-parish; some are admitted on confession and repentance, others delayed or escalated
[img_156].

## Balneil as the purge's central test case

No single figure absorbs more of this purge's energy than **James Ross of Balneil**, whose
own malignancy process runs in parallel — see
[James Ross of Balneil's malignancy process](entity:event/james-ross-of-balneil-s-malignancy-process)
for the full case. His eventual, thoroughly-documented exoneration (session, committee, and
ministers all vouching for him in turn) is arguably the purge's most complete surviving
record of due process actually working in the accused's favour.
