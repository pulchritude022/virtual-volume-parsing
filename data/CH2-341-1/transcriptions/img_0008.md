### Modern English (paraphrase)
[As to Robert Tarbret] he still denied the fact and offered to purge himself by
oath, which session and presbytery would have accepted, but Power moved them to
forbear — so there was no pollution nor cloaking; and even if it were proved (which
it is not), it does not prove that article of the libel (which was laid in the
plural). **(2)** To the charge of being an extorting usurer, he denies it: where
French instances the town of Stranraer, Power answers that he asked the town to
renew his bond bearing the ordinary annualrent, and delivered his old bond to their
clerk; **and that of the interest he received in money since the late Act of
Parliament, he took only "eight for the hundred" (8%)**, and would rather have
taken eight than payment in ware (goods) — so this does not prove usury. **(3)** He
denies being an oppressor; it is not verified by any instance. **(4)** He denies
sowing sedition, and denies the instances alleged by the pursuer. **(5)** To the
charge of ordinary lying and Sabbath-breaking by making merchandise: as to telling
the Provincial Assembly at Wigtown that he had had no means to live on at
Stoneykirk this year past (said to be proven a lie at the Stoneykirk visitation),
he answers he does not remember the exact words used at Wigtown, but the matter is
true even now, and he complained at the said visitation that…

## Notes & context

Both pages are **Mr Gilbert Power's written answers** to French's articles,
continuing from img_0007. He answers the charges in order: adultery-cloaking,
usury (admitting annualrent but "only eight for the hundred" — the legal maximum),
oppression, sedition, ordinary lying, and Sabbath-breaking by "making merchandise"
(which he explains was Michael McIlrae pestering him to sell a mare on a Sabbath
evening).
- **Power's defence, in his own voice.** This is a careful, lawyerly rebuttal of
  French's 18-article libel (img_0007). Highlights:
  - **Usury (art. 2):** Power *admits taking annualrent* but insists he took only
    **"eight for the hundred" (8%)** — the legal ceiling after the mid-17thC
    reduction of lawful annualrent from 10% to 8%. His whole defence is that he
    stayed within the law, not that he lent nothing. This is the crux of the usury
    charge and a nice datapoint on Scottish interest-rate law.
  - **Lying (art. 5):** He argues his poverty claim was *true* — "my stipend was
    not paid… I want the greatest part of two years' stipend."
  - **Legal move:** several instances are dismissed as **"posterior"** to the libel
    (events after the fact cannot prove the original charge).
  - **Sabbath merchandise:** reframed as **Michael McIlrae** importuning *him* to
    sell a mare on a Sabbath evening — Power as reluctant party, not profaner.
- **The accuser's "adherents":** Power names them — **French, Garthland, Kilaster,
  Ardwell, James[?]** — a faction of local lairds ranged against him. Garthland and
  Charles Campbell were the ruling elders he tried to disqualify (img_0006); the
  widow's bond (art. 18) was Campbell's. The case is as much a **local
  laird-vs-minister feud** as a discipline matter.
- **Palaeography applied:** `instruct` = to prove/demonstrate (Scots legal);
  `kythed` = showed/made manifest; `annuelrent` = interest on a loan/wadset;
  `eight for the hundreth` = 8%; `ware` = goods/commodities; `posterior` (Latin) =
  later in time; `sawer of sedition` = sower of discord; `condescend to the
  particular` = specify the detail. `y`+superscript → *th-*.
