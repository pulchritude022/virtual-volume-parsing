The laird whose lands gave the new kirk of Glenluce its name (Balneil = New Luce), whose
money underwrote its stipend for a decade, and who — late in the volume — becomes the
accused in the single most thoroughly documented malignancy case the register contains. Note
that some folios spell the name **Barneill**/**Barneil**; this page merges those variants.

## Patron and creditor of Glenluce

Long before he is ever a defendant, Balneil is Glenluce's financial backbone: he tries (and
fails) to present his own candidate, Mr James Smith, on a 1000-merk mortification in 1642
[img_0044–0045]; he signs an 800-merk bond securing Mr John Crichton's stipend at the new
kirk [img_113–115]; and he is repeatedly chased for **arrears** owed on the Glenluce
mortification, at one point sued directly by Mr George Dick over it [img_0043, img_109–111].
See [The division and planting of Glenluce](entity:event/the-division-and-planting-of-glenluce)
for the full financial saga his money makes possible.

## Accused, and exonerated at every turn

In late 1648 the minister lays nine articles of malignancy against him — commission to
Parliament, a protection from the Engager side, spreading rumour of the English army's
advance. What follows is an unusually complete vindication: his own session, the civil
committee, six lay witnesses, and three ministers (Blair, Crosse, Scott) all independently
corroborate that whatever compliance he showed was reluctant, fear-driven, or technical
rather than genuine malignancy. See
[James Ross of Balneil's malignancy process](entity:event/james-ross-of-balneil-s-malignancy-process)
for the full case — which is, as of the last transcribed folio, still formally open despite
the weight of evidence in his favour.

## A family detail

One of the depositions in his defence happens to record that his **daughter married Major
McDougall** — a genealogical fact surfacing purely as an incidental detail in Mr James
Blair's testimony about where and when he overheard Balneil's private views on the
Engagement [img_161].
