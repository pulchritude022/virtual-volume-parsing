The presbytery's founding drama, and the case that occupies most of the volume's opening
run of folios. **Mr Gilbert Power**, minister of [Stoneykirk](entity:place/stoneykirk), was
accused, tried, deposed, and — after appealing over the presbytery's head — restored by the
General Assembly itself: a rare, well-documented instance of a kirk session and its own
minister's accusers losing at the national level what they had won locally.

## A coordinated accusation

The case opens with **Uchtred McDowell, younger of French**, laying an 18-article libel
against Power: usury, cloaking the adultery of **Robert Tarbret** (with Fergus Cochran's
wife) for a bribe taken via **Ardwell** and **John McCulloch**, Sabbath-breaking, carding
and dicing, perjury, sedition, and — the genealogically loaded charge — falsifying the
testament of the late **Peter McCulloch**, drawing up a second, secret will naming
different executors after the first had already been signed [img_0002, img_0004–0010].
Two more accusers piled on soon after: **Andrew McDowell of Ellaster (Killaster)** and
**James Gordon in Culgroat** [img_0019, img_0025–0026], their charges overlapping closely
enough that Power himself later characterised it as three accusers acting in concert.

Power answered article by article — conceding the 8% annualrent as lawful interest,
reframing the Tarbret payment as an innocent broker's fee — but the presbytery suspended
him from the ministry over the Tarbret point, "agreed by all but two" [img_0012].

## Appeal, back-filling, and a contested chronology

Power did not accept the sentence quietly. He protested, appealed past the presbytery to
the **Synod of Galloway** and ultimately the **General Assembly**, and needled the process
at every turn — objecting to accusers sitting as judges, to no cross-examination being
allowed, to the court refusing him an extract of the proceedings [img_0024]. The
presbytery's own answer to his appeal conceded real irregularities (one member, **Charles
Campbell**, had leaked details "in simplicity"; no woman's testimony had yet been taken)
while still judging the appellation invalid [img_0027–0028].

*A note on the record itself:* the clerkship changed hands mid-case (Livingstone's Irish
relief duties pulled him away; **George Dick** was ordered to back-fill the register from
30 March), and the surviving folios are demonstrably out of date order — a run of "1642"
sederunts sits after material that is textually 1643. The transcription notes flag this
explicitly as unresolved ("REVIEWER MUST RECONCILE"); treat exact month/day dates in this
stretch as provisional until cross-checked. What is not in doubt is the sequence of events:
libel → suspension → deposition → appeal → reposition.

## Deposition, then reversal

The presbytery, joined by commissioners from Kirkcudbright and Wigtown sent by the General
Assembly to help reach "full and final determination," found Power **guilty on a nine-count
charge** (collusion, usury, Sabbath merchandise, perjury, carding, cloaking fornication,
preaching while suspended, falsifying his own session book, sedition) and **deposed** him,
declaring Stoneykirk vacant — "all in one voice, none excepted," Power having been thrice
called and not compearing [img_0041–0042]. Stoneykirk's own kirk session, split between a
pro-Power faction (George McCulloch and six others, [img_0017]) and an official session that
had itself petitioned against him, shows a parish genuinely divided over its minister.

The presbytery did not have the last word. Power's appeal reached the **General Assembly at
St Andrews**, which found the 1642 deposition **null, and null from the beginning**, and
**reponed him to the full and free exercise of his ministry at Stoneykirk** [img_0061] — a
striking rebuke of the local process. The Synod of Galloway and several presbytery members
(Mr George Dick, Mr John McClellan, Mr Hew Henderson as Synod moderator, Uchtred McDowell of
French) resisted honouring the reversal for the best part of two years, keeping Power off
every "approved" list at successive privy trials even while he remained, on paper, a member
[img_0043–0060]. Only in August 1644 did Power force the Assembly's act into the
presbytery's own book and resume the weekly exercise as a full member, approved once more by
Hew Hutcheson [img_0062–0063].

## The final reconciliation (1644)

By his last years Power was old and infirm, and Stoneykirk's session petitioned for a
colleague to help him. Power's own paper of conditions — pay his arrears, let him keep his
whole stipend until a helper arrived, secure his provision after death — was met point by
point by the heritors, who agreed to pay arrears, rejoin him, and repair the decayed kirk
[img_0066–0067]. It is a quietly moving close to a bitter case: even restored and cared for,
Power's own conditions show a man who no longer fully trusted the men who had deposed him.
