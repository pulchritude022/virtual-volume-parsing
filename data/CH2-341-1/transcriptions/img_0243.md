## Notes & context
★★★ **The Dunbar prisoners — a relief collection, receipted into the register.** The most historically striking item in the batch. After the rout at **Dunbar (3 September 1650)**, some five thousand Scots soldiers were marched south as prisoners into England (many dying at Durham). Here the presbytery records **David Kennedy's discharge — receipt — "commissioner … having warrant from the Estates of Parliament," for the contribution collected across all the parishes "for the supply of the poor [Scots prisoners] in England taken at Dunbar in September 1650,"** handed over by moderator **Mr James Blair** and **ordained to be inserted in the register.** The earlier vaguer "contributions for the poor sufferers in England" (imgs 216, 233) are here pinned to the Dunbar captives — a rare parish-level trace of the war's human aftermath and organised relief for the prisoners.

★★ **John Stewart chased from the Synod; the lame-soldiers roll still owing.** The excommunicate/scandalous **John Stewart** (imgs 240–241) surfaces again: **Mr John Crosse dealt with him and Mr William Maxwell at the Synod at Wigton, and is to write to Maxwell to summon Stewart** to the next meeting. And the **lame-soldiers census** (the war-relief roll, imgs 240–242) is **ordered brought yet again** — the brethren perennially behind on it.

★ **Discipline: an impenitent woman and Robert Campbell's refused oath.** **Isobell Murchie** — likely the "Issobell Murdoch" of the 1650 witch/scandal commission (img 200) — is called before the presbytery and **"does not acknowledge, but remains impenitent."** And the Engager-suspect **Robert Campbell "made the refusal of the Bible" — refused to swear the required oath — "for his disobedience,"** so Mr John Dick and Mr John Crosse are set to deal with him. Both cases carried to the next meeting.
