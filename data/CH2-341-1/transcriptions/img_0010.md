### Modern English (paraphrase)
[The £12 from the old bell went] to buying a new bell, which Power sold to
**Archibald Osburne in Ayr for twelve pounds**, sold by weight. He then bought a
new bell from Archibald, brought it home to Stoneykirk, and dealt earnestly with
the parishioners to contribute the rest of the price and for repairing the kirk and
kirkyard; on their refusal he raised **tickets** (assessments — "which are yet
extant") and charged them; but some suspended and others refused absolutely.
Whereupon, on the advice of the late **laird of Garthland**, he sold the new bell
to Garthland's brother-in-law, **the Dean of Raphoe**, for the same price, after it
had lain a year in the kirkyard. The £12 (the old bell's price), by the same advice,
was employed (with more) for repairing the kirk and kirkdyke.

**(13) The contribution for the people of Ayr:** when the brethren delivered their
collections at the first Synod at Wigtown after the **Assembly of Glasgow [1638]**
to **John Knight, Baillie of Ayr**, Power was lying deadly sick and could not
deliver his then; John Knight entreated the Assembly that the contributions of
Glenluce and Kirkpandin[?] (not then ready) with Stoneykirk's (undelivered in
Power's absence) might be given to him later; the Assembly agreed; Power was ready
to give his, which Knight refused except he got the rest as well.

### Modern English
[The bell money] is now forthcoming, to be applied to those people or otherwise as
the presbytery and parish think fitting; so it is no imputation to Power. **(14)
The second perjury** (McHum's oxen): it is *criminal*; he denies it and refers it
to French's proof. **(15) Cloaking fornication**, instanced by **Alexander McDowell
with Jean Hutcheson**: denied. **(16) Elias McFedren** (the eviction): what Power
did was done *legally*, is altogether *civil*, and he can defend himself in law.
**(17) Writing a false testament** (Peter McCulloch's): he wrote his testament
*thrice, at his desire*, but never wrote a false testament, neither for Peter nor
any other; this too is *criminal*, and he humbly craves the presbytery would, for
this and the other criminal points, **remit French to seek reparation against the
persons by the civil law**, since they carry *poenam talionis* (the penalty of the
law of retaliation — false accusation of a crime rebounds on the accuser). **(18)
The widow's evidents** (detaining her title deeds and forcing her, by strong hand,
to dispone her right to Power, and her future spouse to ratify it): a calumny,
merely civil, and he is willing to answer in law. He notes French's inconsistency:
before the presbytery on the third day, French affirmed Power made **Charles
Campbell** and his wife grant a bond of 500 merks "for a hundred"; now French says
Power made *him* grant a bond of 500 merks — "he is not constant"; it is a mere
calumny, denied.

**Conclusion:** Thus, for the presbytery's satisfaction and to clear his own
innocence, Power has answered the particulars, protesting he may have liberty to
enlarge the aforesaid [answers later].

## Notes & context

Left page (folio **9a**) finishes the **kirk-bell** account and answers art. 13
(the collection for Ayr, gathered after the 1638 Assembly of Glasgow). Right page
(folio **10**) **concludes Power's answers** — arts. 14–18 — and his peroration:
for the *criminal* charges he asks the presbytery to remit French to the civil
law, and he catches French contradicting himself on the widow's 500-merk bond.
This closes the block of Power's defence papers (imgs 0007–0010).
- **End of Power's defence papers.** This closes the block (imgs 0007–0010): the
  18-article libel, then Power's article-by-article answers. His overarching legal
  strategy is now explicit — **hive off the "criminal" charges** (perjury, false
  testament, forced bond) to the **civil / criminal courts**, invoking *poenam
  talionis* (a false criminal accusation should rebound on the accuser), while
  answering the "spiritual" charges (usury within 8%, Sabbath, drink, carding) on
  the merits before the presbytery.
- **The kirk bell resolved (art. 12–13):** old broken bell → **Archibald Osburne of
  Ayr** for £12; new bell, unpaid for by the parish, → **the Dean of Raphoe**
  (Garthland's brother-in-law, so a bell went to Ireland); proceeds spent on the
  kirk and kirkdyke. Power frames it all as done on the late laird of Garthland's
  advice.
- **Dateable anchor:** the **"Assembly of Glasgow"** = the famous **1638 General
  Assembly** (which abolished episcopacy); the "first Synod at Wigtown after" it
  places art. 13's events c. 1639, with **John Knight, Baillie of Ayr**, collecting
  for "the people of Ayr."
- **French's credibility dented:** Power catches him shifting the widow's-bond story
  (£500 merks "for a hundred" vs simply 500 merks; wife vs "him") — "he is not
  constant."
- **New instance detail:** the **fornication** Power is accused of cloaking is that
  of **Alexander McDowell with Jean Hutcheson** (art. 15).
- **Palaeography applied:** `tickets` = written assessments/charges on parishioners;
  `dispone` = to convey/make over (property, Scots law); `evidents` = title deeds;
  `by strong hand` = by force; `poenam talionis` (Latin) = the penalty of *lex
  talionis*; `it is criminall`/`meirly civill` = jurisdictional pleas; `not constant`
  = inconsistent. `y`+superscript → *th-*.
