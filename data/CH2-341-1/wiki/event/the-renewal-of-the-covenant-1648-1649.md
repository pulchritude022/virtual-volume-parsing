After the Engagers' defeat at Preston (1648) the triumphant Kirk party did not only punish;
it re-bound the nation to its oaths. The **Solemn Acknowledgment** and a fresh **renewal of
the Solemn League and Covenant** were driven down from the Commission of the General Assembly
into every presbytery, and the Stranraer register records the drive in detail — the
constructive, oath-taking side of the same movement whose punitive side was the
[post-Engagement purge](entity:event/the-post-engagement-purge-of-malignants).

## Materials from the Commission of the Kirk

The Commission of the Kirk sends the presbytery its papers for **renewing the Covenant**,
together with the Large and Short Declarations and the Solemn Acknowledgment [img_150–151].
The presbytery first examines *itself* — the brethren making their own solemn acknowledgment
before administering it more widely — then carries the renewal out into the parishes.

## Debarring the compliers

The renewal is deliberately gated. Every session is charged to **search out Engagement
compliers** and report them, so that they may be **debarred from the Covenant** until they
give satisfaction to the Kirk [img_162–164]. Sessions examine their own heritors
parish-by-parish; some men are received on an "ingenuous confession" and repentance, others
delayed for further trial, others escalated [img_156]. The oath thus becomes both a spiritual
act and a loyalty filter.

## The gentry bound at Glenluce

The most concrete surviving trace of enforcement is the repeated deposition that the
"gentlemen of the shire were bound at [Glenluce](entity:place/glenluce), by a motion of [the
Marquess of] Argyll's" — a mass subscription of the National Covenant under the direct
pressure of the Kirk party's greatest noble patron [img_161]. It shows exactly how, on the
ground, a national renewal was made to happen.
