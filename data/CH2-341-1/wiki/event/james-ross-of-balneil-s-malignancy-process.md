The most fully documented single case in the volume's later run — nine articles of
malignancy, a point-by-point defence, three independent rounds of corroborating testimony,
and, even after all of that, no final verdict yet visible in the transcribed folios. It
doubles as the fullest surviving illustration of how [the post-Engagement
purge](entity:event/the-post-engagement-purge-of-malignants) actually worked case by case.

## The nine articles (Nov 1648)

The minister lays nine articles of malignancy against **James Ross of Balneil**: that he
held a commission to Parliament at Michaelmas, refused to sign an anti-Engagement
supplication, advised **Major McDougall**, took a protection and refused to pay his share of
the levy, pressed the reading of the Estates' observations, "spread civil novels" (rumour),
showed disaffection and conversed with malignants, and pressed a man into service who then
fled [img_153–155]. Balneil's own point-by-point answers deny malignancy outright while
conceding "breaches of the covenant": his protection, he says, came from the Earl of
Callendar, not Duke Hamilton; quartering was unequally distributed; his only "civil novel"
was talk of the prevailing of the English army. His own kirk session judges that he
"ingenuously declared the truth" — substantially clearing himself at the first hurdle
[img_154–155].

## An unusually thorough vindication (Dec 1648 – Jan 1649)

What follows is remarkable for how many independent bodies are asked to weigh in, and how
consistently they land in Balneil's favour:

- The **civil committee** reports it has "nothing to say against Balneil" and that the
  presbytery may proceed without waiting on it further [img_158].
- **Lay witnesses** — Major McDougall, John Kevan, Robert Gunnokat, John McMillan, William
  Gordon, John McDougall — depose that he never pressed the Engagement on his tenants, telling
  them "if any of you will not join, I am content, I will bear with you" [img_160].
- The **ministers themselves** vouch for him: Mr James Blair saw him subscribe the National
  Covenant at the Glenluce gentry-binding and heard him privately call the Engagement
  unlawful; Mr John Crosse heard him express dislike of it; Mr John Scott says any
  compliance was "for fear of the shire, not out of any good affection" [img_161].

By this point Balneil has been cleared by his session, the civil committee, lay witnesses,
and the presbytery's own ministers — session, state, and clergy all in agreement.

## Referred up, then referred back down (1649)

Rather than closing the case on this evidence, the presbytery **refers Balneil's process
up** to the Commission of the Kirk, because an existing Commission act is "not fully plain"
on whether holding a parliamentary commission automatically debars a man from the
eldership [img_162]. The Commission sends it back "not satisfied," prompting Balneil to
**petition the presbytery directly** to be reponed to the eldership, protesting he has been
kept out of it "to the grit grief & prejudice of your supplicant" and looked on as suspect
[img_165]. A reading of his supplication as containing a reflection on the presbytery's own
commissioner (Mr Alexander Turnbull) draws a formal admonition rather than a resolution
[img_166].

## Still open at the edge of the transcribed record

The last folios transcribed so far (imgs 167–168) show the case still unresolved: Balneil
is reported "out of the countrie" with the process papers in his own custody, so the
committee that was supposed to forward them never did; and the presbytery draws up a formal
**probation checklist** — an article-by-article list of exactly which "presumptions"
against him remain uncleared, down to hunting for corroborating "speeches at the Earl of
[—]'s table" via a Wigtown correspondent [img_167–168]. Whatever the eventual outcome, the
process illustrates a presbytery working scrupulously through a genuinely ambiguous case
rather than rubber-stamping either conviction or acquittal.
