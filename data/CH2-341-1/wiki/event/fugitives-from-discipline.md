A recurring — and very human — pattern in the register is **flight**. Faced with a summons to
answer for adultery, fornication, or a graver crime, a striking number of people simply
**leave the bounds** rather than compear. The presbytery's efforts to trace, cite, and reclaim
them are among the most vivid traces in the book of both the reach and the limits of church
discipline.

## Across the water to Ireland

By far the commonest escape is across the North Channel — see
[The Ulster connection](entity:event/the-ulster-connection-letters-across-the-north-channel).
Again and again an accused offender is reported to have "**fled to Ireland**," and the
presbytery writes after them, seeks testimonials of their conduct abroad, or corresponds with
Ulster brethren to have them dealt with there. Portpatrick's short crossing made the border
porous, and everyone knew it.

## Traced, pursued, sometimes returned

Not all fugitives get clean away. The court sends citations, raises a "hue and cry" through
neighbouring presbyteries, traces absconders to specific places (one is followed as far as
Douglas), and occasionally a runaway **returns to repentance** and is received back on public
satisfaction. The machinery is dogged, if not always successful.

## What it reveals

These cases show discipline as a system with holes in it — and show ordinary people exercising
a real, if desperate, agency against it. For a family historian, a "fled to Ireland" entry can
be the last Scottish trace of an ancestor before they vanish across the sea.

## Context

Fugitive cases overlap heavily with the sexual-discipline business and with the
[Ulster connection](entity:event/the-ulster-connection-letters-across-the-north-channel);
together they map the geography of escape from a 17th-century Scottish parish.
