The last great controversy of the volume is the fracture that split the whole Church of
Scotland in two. After the catastrophe at [Dunbar](entity:event/the-dunbar-prisoners-relief)
(September 1650), the Commissions of the General Assembly passed the **Public Resolutions**,
readmitting former royalists and Engagers — the very "malignants" the Kirk had spent 1648–49
[purging](entity:event/the-post-engagement-purge-of-malignants) — to the army and to office,
on the argument that the nation needed every hand against the English. A minority
**protested** that this was a betrayal of the Covenant. The two parties — **Resolutioners**
and **Protesters** — never fully reconciled, and the Stranraer register catches the quarrel
playing out as day-to-day procedure inside one small presbytery.

## Turnbull's protestation

The local standard-bearer of the Protester cause is **[Mr Alexander
Turnbull](entity:person/mr-alexander-turnbull)**, who dissents from the presbytery's approval
of the late General Assembly and demands that his protestation be entered "under the clerk's
hand" in the register [img_247, img_251, img_255]. The presbytery, uneasy, tries to have the
paper *considered* but *not inserted* until after the Synod — the Synod being the body meant
"for the transacting of differences amongst brethren" [img_256–257].

## The brethren split three ways

A conference called to resolve the matter only exposes how deep the division runs: the
brethren "were not all of one judgment." **Turnbull** counts it "a matter of conscience" and
"could neither come nor go" upon the paper; **[Mr James Lawrie](entity:person/mr-james-lawrie)**
wants to re-argue "the whole matter from the beginning"; and **[Mr John
Park](entity:person/mr-john-park)** states his own qualified position [img_255–256]. That
Park and Lawrie appear alongside the Protester Turnbull is one of the puzzles the record
leaves for a reviewer — elsewhere both act on the Resolutioner side — and the exact alignment
is flagged as uncertain in the page notes.

## "Insert it, or all buried"

The dispute never resolves inside the volume. At the very last sitting (14 April 1652) the
presbytery "unwillingly, and of one accord" agrees only to defer inserting either the
Protesters' paper or its own answer until after the Synod; and Turnbull makes his final
stand, demanding that "the whole relation of their business should be inserted in the
register, **or all buried**," and protesting against the delay [img_257]. The register then
closes, the schism still open and referred upward — and it is the Synod clerk **Robert
Ferguson**, not the presbytery, who has the last word, signing off his revision of the book
on 8 June 1652.
