On 3 September 1650 Cromwell shattered the Scots army at **Dunbar**, taking thousands of
prisoners — many of whom were marched south in appalling conditions. In the aftermath the
Kirk organised charitable **collections for the relief of the prisoners taken at Dunbar**,
and the Stranraer presbytery ran its own contribution through the bounds, tracked across the
volume's 1650–52 openings.

## A collection across the bounds

Like the earlier gatherings for the Irish refugees and for Argyll, the Dunbar relief is
administered parish by parish: sums are called in from the sessions, accounted, and forwarded
through an appointed hand. The business recurs as an item of ordinary court administration
across several sittings, sitting alongside the poor-relief and
[widows-and-orphans](entity:event/the-poor-the-widows-and-the-orphans-of-war) rolls that the
war years had made a permanent feature of the register.

## Captain David Kennedie winds it up

The contribution is eventually closed out, with **Captain David Kennedie** accounting for and
delivering the collected money as the business is wound up in early 1652 [img_251]. It is one
of the last pieces of "national" charitable administration in the book before the
[union crisis](entity:event/the-cromwellian-conquest-and-the-tender-of-union) and the
[schism](entity:event/the-resolutioner-protester-schism) dominate the closing folios.

## Context

The relief is a small but telling window on how a defeat two hundred miles away reached down
into a remote presbytery as a concrete administrative task — money to be gathered from
Galloway parishes for men taken at a battle most of the contributors would never have seen.
