# Reading Guide: Scottish Secretary Hand & Scots (c. 1560–1750)

A working reference for transcribing **Stranraer Presbytery minutes, CH2/341/1 (1641–1652)** and
similar Church of Scotland court records. Distilled from the National Records of Scotland
"Scottish Handwriting" tutorial (scotlandspeople.gov.uk/scottish-handwriting) plus standard
palaeography. This file is fed to the transcription model as priming context — keep it practical.

> Golden rule from the NRS tutorial: **intuition is a starting point, never a conclusion.**
> Always check the word you *think* you see letter-by-letter and confirm the spelling. A word is
> either (a) a familiar word spelled unusually, or (b) an unfamiliar word that merely looks familiar.

---

## 1. What this document is

- **Record type:** Presbytery minutes — the court above the kirk session. Each meeting ("sederunt")
  records who was present (the *members* / ministers + ruling elders), business, discipline cases,
  witness depositions, and decisions ("ordanit", "referrit", "continewit").
- **Languages:** Predominantly **Scots** (not English — a distinct language), with **Latin**
  formulae for headings, dates, and legal phrases. Expect code-switching mid-sentence.
- **Era:** Covenanter period. Strict Presbyterian discipline. Cases of fornication, adultery,
  slander ("scandall"), sabbath-breaking, and property/tithe disputes are common.
- **Hand:** **Secretary Hand** — an administrative "shorthand" used across western Europe
  1500–1750. Letter forms differ sharply from modern writing and **must be memorised**, not guessed.

## 2. The two make-or-break letters (tutorial Step 2)

- **`e`** — looks like the Greek letter **theta (θ)**: a loop with a horizontal bar. It is the most
  common vowel, so locking this in dramatically speeds reading. Do **not** read every θ-shape as `o`.
- **`s`** comes in two forms:
  - **long `s` (ſ)** — descends, looks like an `f` without the full crossbar; **used at the start
    and in the middle** of words. `ſ` + `s` doubling appears mid-word.
  - **short/round `s`** — sits on the line; **used at the ends** of words.
  - Mnemonic word from the tutorial: *scandalous* shows long-s at the front, round-s near the end.

## 3. Other confusable letters (tutorial Step 3)

| Letter | Form & trap |
|---|---|
| `a` | Like a modern *handwritten* (not printed) `a`, with a diagonal "attacking stroke" from bottom-left up to top-right. e.g. *thame*. |
| `b` | Like a modern **capital** `B`. e.g. *box*, *breithren*. |
| `c` | Two sides of a square, or like a modern `r`; the vertical is often curved/diagonal. **Easily confused with `t` and `r`.** |
| `d` | Near-modern, but **dangerously similar to Secretary `e`** — check context. Often has a looped ascender leaning left. |
| `h` | Like a **butcher's hook**, descends below the line. Sloppy at word-ends. e.g. *hundreth*, *schilling*. |
| `r` | Two forms: **curly `r`** (built of ~3 strokes) and **descending `r`** (3 descending diagonals). |
| `t` | Very like `c`: a vertical + a horizontal stroke; top of the vertical tends to curl right. `c` vs `t` is the classic ambiguity. |

Additional standard forms not in the summary but expected in this hand:
- **`p`** with the descender sometimes looped; **`p` with a bar through the descender = "per/par"**,
  **`p` with a curl = "pro"**, **`p` with superscript = "pre"** (Latin brevigraphs, see §5).
- **`w`** often written like modern `w` but can resemble ` br`/`bi`.
- **Minims** (the identical vertical strokes making up `i n u m`) run together: *minister* can look
  like a picket fence. Count strokes and use context.
- **Capital letters** are highly decorative and inconsistent; don't rely on capitalisation for
  proper nouns or sentence starts.

## 4. `y` = thorn (þ) — the single most useful trick

Scribes used **`y` as the old letter thorn (þ = "th")**, usually with a superscript letter:

| Written | Read as | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| `y` + superscript `e` (`yᵉ`) | **þe** | *the* |
| `yt` / `yᵗ` | **þat** | *that* |
| `yis` | **þis** | *this* |
| `yem` / `ym` | **þem** | *them* |
| `yr` / `yᵣ` | **þair / þere** | *their / there* |
| `yairefter` | | *thereafter* |

Do **not** transcribe these as literal "ye / yat". Expand to *the / that*, and (optionally) note the
original form. `yᵉ` is **not** the archaic pronoun "ye".

## 5. Abbreviations, contractions & superscripts

Very heavy in court records. Common ones:

- **`Mr`** = *Maister* (a graduate/minister — a status marker, not just "Mister").
- **`ye / yt / yis / ym / yr`** = the / that / this / them / their (see §4).
- **`wt`** = *with*; **`wch` / `qch` / `quhilk`** = *which*; **`qᵘ` / `quh-`** = the Scots **`quh`**
  cluster (see §6) → *quhilk, quhen, quha* = which, when, who.
- **`sd` / `sᵈ`** = *said*; **`aforesd`** = aforesaid.
- **`umqle` / `umquhile`** = *the late / deceased* (before a name).
- **`comperit / compeirit`** = appeared (in court); **`delatit`** = accused/reported; **`ordanit`**
  = ordained/ordered; **`continewit`** = adjourned/postponed; **`referrit`** = referred.
- **`&c`** = etcetera; **`p`-brevigraphs** = per/par/pro/pre (§3).
- **Superscript letters** generally mark a contraction: the raised letter is the ending
  (`Wᵐ` = William, `Jhonᵉ`/`Jᵒ` = John, `Alexʳ` = Alexander, `Dᵒ` = Ditto/the same).
- **Tilde / macron (a horizontal line over a letter)** = omitted `m`, `n`, or other letters:
  `comissioun` written `comissiõun`; `presbrie`/`presbie` with a stroke = *presbyterie*.
- **Tironian sign / `7`-like mark** = *and* (Latin *et*).

## 6. Scots spelling & vocabulary (tutorial Step 4)

Standardised spelling is an **18th-century invention**; before ~1750 expect phonetic, variable
spelling — the **same word spelled several ways on one page**, especially names.

Sound/spelling conventions:
- **`quh-`** where modern English has **`wh-`**: *quhilk* (which), *quha* (who), *quhen* (when),
  *quhat* (what), *umquhile* (late/deceased).
- **`sch-`** for **`sh-`**: *schilling*, *scho* (she), *parochin* (parish).
- **`-it`** for past tense **`-ed`**: *ordanit, delatit, comperit, confessit*.
- **`ane`** = *a / one* (indefinite article): *ane elder*, *ane act*.
- **`v`/`u`/`w`** interchange: *loue* (love), *vp* (up), *woman/voman*.
- **`i`/`y`** interchange; **`-ie`/`-y`** endings: *abilitie, presbyterie*.
- Doubled/odd vowels for length: *improove, foaneticallie, contayneing* (= containing),
  *breithren* (brethren), *hundreth* (hundred).
- **`signe`** (not "figure"); watch the long-s at the front.

Church-court vocabulary you will meet constantly:
- **kirk** (church), **kirk session**, **presbyterie**, **synod**, **moderator**, **clerk**,
  **elder / ruling elder**, **minister**, **beddell/beadle**, **parochin/parish**.
- **compeir** (appear before the court), **delate** (accuse/report), **dittay** (charge/indictment),
  **sederunt** (those present / the sitting), **quorum**.
- **scandall / slander**, **fornicatioun**, **adulterie**, **sabbath breaking**, **cursing**,
  **flyting** (quarrelling).
- **repentance / stuill of repentance** (stool of repentance), **sacco / sackcloth**,
  **censure**, **excommunicatioun**, **absolvit**, **rebuikit** (rebuked), **admonischit**.
- **penult / ultimo / instant** for dates (see §7); **merk / mark**, **pund** (£ Scots),
  **schilling**, **boll** (grain measure) for money and rents.

## 7. Dates, numerals & money

- Dates often **Latin**: *die* (day), *mensis* (month), regnal/AD years; weekdays in Scots
  (*wonsday* = Wednesday, *thurisday*, *setterday*).
- **`instant`** = of the current month; **`ultimo`** = of last month; **`penult`** = second-last day.
- Numbers frequently **Roman numerals**, often lowercase with a terminal `j`: *iiij* = 4, *vij* = 7,
  *xij* = 12, *xxti* = 20. Also written as Scots words (*fyftein, tuentie, threttie*).
- **Money is £ Scots** (≈ 1/12 £ sterling): *merk* = 13s 4d (⅔ of a pund); *pund*, *schilling* (s),
  *penny/d*. "100 merks" appears in the very first pages (the MacCulloch case).

## 9. Transcription conventions to follow (output contract)

When transcribing, produce a **diplomatic** reading (faithful to the page) plus a **modern**
rendering. In the diplomatic layer:
- Keep original spelling, including `quh-`, `sch-`, `-it`, `ane`, doubled vowels.
- **Expand** superscript/brevigraph contractions with the added letters in *[square brackets]*,
  e.g. `Mr` → `M[aiste]r`, `ye` → `[the]`, `wt` → `w[i]t[h]`, `sd` → `s[ai]d`. (Or, if noise, expand
  silently and note the convention once.)
- Mark **uncertain readings** with `[?]` after the word, and **illegible** stretches as `[illeg.]`
  or `[…]` with an approximate word count.
- Note **line breaks** (`/`) and **page/folio boundaries** — remember **each image is a two-page
  opening**, so label the left and right pages separately (e.g. *folio 1r* / *folio 2r*).
- Flag **marginalia**, **catchwords** (a word at the foot of a page repeating the next page's first
  word), **insertions/deletions**, and **damage/torn edges** (this MS has ragged, stained margins).
- Preserve **proper nouns exactly as written**, then give the normalised modern form in the modern
  layer (e.g. *Stranrawer* → Stranraer).

## 10. Quick sanity checklist before finalising a page

1. Did I read every `y`+superscript as *th-*, not "ye/yat"?
2. Did I resolve `c` vs `t` and `e` vs `d` by context, not first impression?
3. Are the **names** among the §8 list, or plausibly new — and spelled as on the page?
4. Are **numbers/dates/money** internally consistent (a merk = 13s 4d, £ Scots)?
5. Have I marked what I could **not** read rather than inventing plausible text?
