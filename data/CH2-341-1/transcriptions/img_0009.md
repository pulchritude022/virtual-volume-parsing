### Modern English (paraphrase)
[The mare story concludes:] Power told McIlrae, not now, but come another day and
you shall have her as we can agree; McIlrae presently left; whether he offered
money Power does not remember, but he is certain he made no bargain that (Sabbath)
day — it was the working day afterward that McIlrae came, agreed, and received the
mare. **(6) Engrossing victual** and exacting great prices of the poor: denied; as
to Francis Hay (that Power bought victual from him about 1638), denied — Hay, being
then but a child, had none to sell; nor does the accuser name the poor from whom
prices were exacted. **(7) Drunkenness** (July 1640): denied — the instance is too
general, naming neither place nor company, and proves nothing. **(8) Dicing and
carding:** he *confesses* that sometimes, at gentlemen's desire, he recreated, but
very seldom, and for their pleasure, not for gain; what he lost was little, what he
won he restored to them or their servants; and he has not done so these seven years,
so it does not make him a "frequenter." **(9) Detractor and calumniator:** denied.
As to the instance that he told the council at Edinburgh that "the Sheriff elder and
Logan suborned the witnesses" he had summoned to prove French's injury to him —
which French terms a calumny…

### Modern English
**(9, continued)** It was no calumny but a real deed that was felt. And where
French says Power named "the Sheriff elder" as suborning them — Power never
mentioned the Sheriff's name; but when he could get no redress of the injury he
sustained, the Chancellor told him the witnesses had proven nothing. Power replied
he could prove presently, by witnesses within the house, that these witnesses had
confessed **French gave him a cuff (blow) so that his hat fell off**; and he added
he was informed that **Logan had dealt (or caused deal) with the witnesses to
depone favourably** — which was no calumny, since he spoke according to his
information. As to the other instance (that Power said French had no just cause to
quarrel his ministry but only through hereditary malice): though the malice be
apparent, the words of Power's appellation were that French **is his hereditary
accuser**, since French's father accused Power before, as French himself and the
first part of the libel testify; [and this instance cannot] prove [the article],
being *posterior* to the libel. **(10) Perjury before Captain Maxwell:** denied —
let French use any instance he can; and it is a *criminal* matter [not for the
presbytery]. **(11):** deserves no answer. **(12) The kirk bell:** though French
cannot prove Power meddled with the bell, yet, for the presbytery's satisfaction,
Power relates the truth — the bell, in ringing, fell; the hanger of the tongue
(clapper) broke, and it was sent by the (late) laird of Garthland, "of good
memory," to **William Guthrie, smith**, to repair it anew; and the said late laird
of Garthland directed Power to sell it to some merchant for a help (toward funds)…

## Notes & context

Both pages continue **Mr Gilbert Power's answers** to French's libel (arts. 6–12),
and are comparatively legible. Notable: he dismisses the butter-engrossing charge
(Francis Hay "was but a child" in 1638), *confesses* to occasional dicing and
carding "for gentlemen's pleasure, not for gain," recasts the calumny charge, and
tells the story of the **Stoneykirk kirk bell** that broke while ringing.
- **Power's rebuttal continues**, arts. 6–12. He is methodical: deny + discredit
  the instance (Francis Hay "but a child"; the drink charge "but a general");
  concede the small stuff frankly (dicing/carding "for their pleasure, not for
  gain," not in seven years); and push jurisdiction (perjury before Captain Maxwell
  is "criminal" — a matter for the civil court, not the presbytery).
- **A vivid detail:** French allegedly **"gave me a cuff whill my hatt fell off"** —
  a physical altercation underlies the "calumny" article. And the feud is
  **hereditary**: "his father did accuse me before" — French is Power's "hereditary
  accuser."
- **The kirk bell (art. 12):** the bell of Stoneykirk broke in ringing (the clapper's
  hanger snapped) and was sent for repair to **William Guthrie, smith**, on the
  direction of the late **laird of Garthland** — who also directed Power to sell it
  to a merchant "for a help." So the "meddling with the bell" charge concerns
  disposal of broken church metal, done (Power says) on the laird's instruction.
- **New figures:** **the Chancellor** (of Scotland — the case had reached national
  church/state attention via the Synod/Assembly), **William Guthrie** (smith),
  **the Sheriff (elder)** and **McDowell of Logan** (the alleged witness-tamperers).
- **Palaeography applied:** `cuff` = a blow; `whill` = until/while; `throw` =
  through; `hinger of the tongue` = the hanger of the (bell) clapper; `of good
  memorie` / `umwhyle` = the late/deceased; `posterior` (Latin) = later; `it is
  criminall` = it is a matter for the criminal (civil) court. `y`+superscript → *th-*.
