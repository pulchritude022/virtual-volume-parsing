Not every scandal before the presbytery was sexual or political. A steady undercurrent of the
volume is **violence** — duels, "combats," assaults, robberies, and oppressions — brought
before the court either as sins requiring repentance or as crimes to be pursued jointly with
the civil magistrate. Together they open a window onto the ordinary turbulence of Galloway
life in a hard decade.

## Duels and combats

The record repeatedly deals with **single combats and challenges** among the gentry and their
followers — affairs of honour that the Kirk treated as grave scandals demanding public
satisfaction, since a duel set private vengeance above both law and gospel. The disciplining
of a "combat" shows the church court attempting to hold a violent honour-culture to account.

## The robbery of the English traveller

The single most followed violent case is the **robbery of a "poor stranger" (an Englishman)**,
pursued across several sittings until it is pinned on **[Thomas
Baillie](entity:person/thomas-baillie), son to John Baillie in Laigh Cross**, who is cited
before the Committee of War as well as the presbytery [img_194]. It is a good example of the
overlap between church and civil justice in a period when the two systems worked hand in hand.

## Assault, oppression, and tumult

Lesser violence recurs throughout: assaults, the **oppression** of the weak by the powerful
(a landlord against a widow, a creditor against a debtor), and outright **tumult** — most
memorably the "tumult of Stranraer" in the volume's opening folios [img_3], where a crowd,
including women, disrupts the court's business and is itself brought to submission.

## Context

These cases rarely make the headline narrative, but they are where the register touches the
raw texture of daily life — the quarrels, blows, and thefts of a small society, recorded
because the kirk claimed jurisdiction over them.
