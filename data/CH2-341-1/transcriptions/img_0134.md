# CH2/341/1 — Image 134 (folios 133v–134)

Both pages continue and **conclude the letter from the Commissioners of the Kirk
(Edinburgh, 28 April 1648)** that began on image 133, presented at the **Inch
sitting of 17 May 1648**. It is a political circular of the Engagement crisis: the
Commission recounts its dealings with the Estates of Parliament over religion and
the **breaches of the treaties and the Covenant**; it declined to give its
judgment on Parliament's declaration until its own former desires were satisfied;
it protests against Parliament proceeding to determine matters of religion
"without the advice and consent of the kirk," notes that **several members of
Parliament dissented and protested for the liberty of the kirk**, and resolves to
send demands to the **Parliament of England**. The close warns that ★ **malignants
and disaffected men are endeavouring — by subtle means and fair speeches on the
one hand, and false reports on the other — to seduce some of the ministry against
the kirk and the cause of God**, whereby the liberties of the kirk and the
ordinances of Christ would be weakened; and exhorts the brethren, after so many
mercies and deliverances, to adhere firmly to the Covenant.

### Modern English *(sense of the letter's close)*
[...before our advice and consent should be given, the Estates recommended that we
return our opinion in answer to their declarations — issued the former way, both
to the King and the Parliament of England — and to their proceeding upon the
liberty of the kirk by taking resolutions in matters of religion without us. We
delayed to give any judgment thereof until our former desires should first be
satisfied, entreating their Lordships to communicate the whole matter to us, that
we might see all things carried on to the furthering of religion and preserving
the ancient bond between the kingdoms. We presented an humble petition, pressing
an answer to our former desires and a remonstrance of [the state] of the kirk of
Scotland if they should refuse to hearken. Their Lordships, after four days,
communicated a declaration containing the grounds and resolutions of Parliament,
with another paper answering our eight desires; and as to the demand concerning
religion, [said] that in that part which concerns religion they would be ready to
take the advice of the Commissioners of the kirk. But after reading the
declarations — which gave no satisfaction to our first desires — we [protested]
(as we had already done in a paper of the 25th of this instant) against Parliament
proceeding to determine in matters of the kirk, and highly [touching] religion,
without the advice and consent of the kirk. Many considerable members of
Parliament dissented and protested against the same, for the liberty of the kirk;
and we found ourselves necessitated to protest against the prejudicing of the
liberties of the kirk, according to the word of God... We are not ignorant of the
designs of **malignants and disaffected men, who endeavour nothing more diligently
than, if possible, by subtle means and fair speeches on the one hand and slander
by false reports on the other, to win over some of the ministry against the kirk
and cause of God**, whereby the liberties of the kirk and the ordinances of Christ
would be weakened. These things we write, not as if we were afraid of any of you,
our dear brethren; we hope that, after so many mercies and deliverances, every
minister of Christ throughout the land will adhere firmly [to the Covenant]... and
we make no question of your constancy, and that they will be ready to...] (the
letter continues onto the next opening).

## Notes & context
- ★ **The Engagement crisis in the presbytery register.** This copied letter is
  the Commission of the Kirk's protest against the **Engagers'** Parliament (spring
  1648) determining matters of religion without the kirk's consent — the ecclesiastical
  half of the split that led to the Duke of Hamilton's ill-fated invasion of
  England (August 1648) and its defeat at Preston. That the Stranraer presbytery
  copies it in full shows the national politics reaching parish level.
- ★ **"Malignants and disaffected men" seducing ministers.** The letter's rhetoric
  — malignants working "by subtle means and fair speeches on the one side, and
  slander by false reports on the other" to win over ministers — is the standard
  Covenanter framing of the Engager party; useful context for the volume's
  recurring references to malignants (e.g. the Old-Glenluce gentry's protest that
  "none are known to be malignants" in the bounds, img 123).
- This opening carries no new local names/cases; it is documentary background. The
  local business (Ardwell, the paternity case, the Glenluce charges, the Dick/
  Livingstone transportations) resumes on the following opening.
