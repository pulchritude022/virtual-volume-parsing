Running underneath the volume's discipline of fornication and malignancy is a thinner but
striking thread: the court's dealings with **witchcraft, charming, and superstition.** In
Covenanting Scotland witch-belief was not a fringe matter but a live concern of the church
courts, and the Stranraer register preserves several windows onto popular belief beneath the
official religion.

## Commissions and trials

Witchcraft was a capital crime tried by civil commission, but the church courts were the
usual place a suspicion first surfaced and the body that pressed for a **commission** to be
sought from the Privy Council or the justices. The volume records the presbytery corresponding
over witch-trials and referring suspects onward — the ecclesiastical front end of a machinery
whose sharp end lay with the civil magistrate.

## "Turning the riddle"

The most vivid single item is a case of folk-divination: **"turning the riddle"** — a sieve
suspended on a pair of shears and read for its movement to identify a thief or answer a
question — prosecuted as superstition and charming [img_236]. It is exactly the kind of
everyday cunning-practice that rarely reaches any written record, and it survives here only
because the kirk treated it as a scandal to be disciplined.

## Why it matters

For a family or local historian these entries are gold: they name ordinary people — often
women — and the small magical practices of Galloway life that the discipline system, almost
incidentally, wrote down. See also the [glossary](glossary.html) entry on *turning the riddle*.

## Context

The witchcraft business sits alongside the court's other work of policing belief and behaviour;
it never dominates a sitting, but its recurrence across the decade shows how seriously a
presbytery took its remit over the invisible as well as the visible sins of its bounds.
