## Folio 235 (right page)  *(confidence: medium)*

*Mr John Park reports he wrote to the Inch session and received their undertaking to attend; and **four Inch heritors compear — Gilbert Hutchison of Craigcaffie and his son young Craigcaffie, John Carlisle of Conote, and David Kennedy of Killern (factor to my Lord of Cassillis) — intreating the presbytery to concur with them "for using some effectual means for retaining their minister Mr John Dick." The presbytery grants their desire and appoints its GA commissioners to concur with them at the General Assembly.** **John Campbell compears at last and excuses his absence "because he was on his way to the army,"** and is set to satisfy the next presbytery day. ### Modern English

## Notes & context
★★★ **Inch's laird-heritors fight to keep Mr John Dick — and the presbytery takes their side to the Assembly.** The transportation struggle (imgs 227–234) reaches a head: Park's letter draws a written undertaking from the Inch session, and **four named heritors appear in person** — **Gilbert Hutchison of Craigcaffie and his son ("young Craigcaffie"), John Carlisle of Conote, and David Kennedy of Killern, factor to the Earl of Cassillis** — "intreating the presbytery to concur … for retaining their minister Mr John Dick." **The presbytery grants it and directs its own General Assembly commissioners to press the case at the Assembly.** A rare, vivid picture of a rural congregation's gentry mobilising to block a ministerial transfer, and the presbytery carrying the fight upward.

★★ **John Campbell's excuse — "on his way to the army."** The defiant Ireland-malignant (imgs 216–234) finally compears and pleads a striking excuse for missing the stool: **he "was on his way to the army."** In the summer of 1651, under the **Public Resolutions** that readmitted former Engagers to the forces against Cromwell (img 234), the notorious malignant's road to rehabilitation runs literally through the levy — he is given one more chance to satisfy. A neat human illustration of the Resolutioner policy in action.

★ **A lame soldier relieved; commissioners named.** **John McKelly, "a lame soldier" of Stoneykirk**, is granted relief out of the parish's soldiers' contribution — the war's human cost handled at the parish level. The presbytery names its **commissioners to the next General Assembly** (Turnbull, Blair, and a ruling elder, the goodman of Park), and distributes the Commission's exhortation-papers "printed at Stirling."
