## Notes & context
★★★ **22 July 1650 — the Kirk mobilising against Cromwell, on the very day of the invasion.** The dating is arresting: at a *pro re nata* sitting **at Glenluce on 22 July 1650 — the day Cromwell's army crossed the Tweed into Scotland** — the presbytery receives a battery of national directives for the war effort: **(1) a written letter from the General Assembly to "correspond with the Shire for the outputting of the Levy" and that "no scandalous person be admitted to the army";** (2) a **printed letter with the Commission/Assembly's resolution that "the ministry of the kingdom and other well-affected offered to contribute for the outputting of a regiment of horse, as a testimony of their zeal in opposing the [enemy]";** (3) a **letter from the Presbytery of Edinburgh** to the same purpose; and (4) **"9 public warnings subscribed by the moderator of the Commission"** to be read from the pulpits. This is the Rhins end of the **great 1650 mobilisation and army-purge** — the "outputting of the Levy," the insistence that "no scandalous person be admitted to the army" (the purging that would help doom the Covenanter host), and the ministers' own subscription regiment — six weeks before **Dunbar (3 September 1650)**. A first-rank national-history anchor.

★★ **The Balneil case answered — and a new charge.** The presbytery finishes rebutting Balneil (imgs 201–202): he alone was pursued (no one else escaped notice); his trouble at the **Committee at Kirkcowan** was self-inflicted ("he left the committee, refusing to take the oath and sit"); there is "no new accusation since his own confession"; and the delay "should always come from himself, and never from the presbytery." Meanwhile **Gilbert Kennedy of Aviolen** — the duellist of imgs 188–196 — is now hit with **a common report of adultery**, referred to the sessions of Inch, Leswalt, and Stranraer for enquiry.

★ **Local business.** The **Portpatrick/Argrenan perambulation** dispute goes to a meeting at **Balgregan** (Monday), with power to order a fresh measuring if the arbiters cannot agree.
