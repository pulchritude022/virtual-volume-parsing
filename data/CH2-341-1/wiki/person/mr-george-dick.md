Clerk of the presbytery during the pivotal 1642 stretch of the Power case, later a
moderator, and one of the men Power specifically accused (via Turnbull and Livingstone) of
taking bribes from Ardwell and Killaster — an allegation the presbytery did not uphold.

## Backfilling the register

When Livingstone left abruptly for Ireland with a run of acts still unentered, George Dick
was ordered to **back-fill the register** from 30 March 1642 forward — which is exactly why
several folios in this stretch of the volume are demonstrably out of chronological order:
the physical page sequence follows when Dick wrote each sederunt up, not when it happened
[img_0029]. Anyone dating folios in the early-1640s run should treat this as the reason for
apparent date jumps.

## Glenluce's stand-in minister-broker

During Power's suspension, the presbytery tries to route Glenluce's stalled provision
through Dick — sending him to Edinburgh, requiring him to renounce manse and glebe and take
only the mortification — though it is unclear from the surviving folios whether he ever
formally became Glenluce's minister [img_0044–0045]. He is also sued by James Rosse (of
Balneil) over the Glenluce mortification directly [img_0043].

## A named resister of Power's reposition

After the General Assembly reponed Power, George Dick is one of the named local figures
(with Mr John McClellan, Hew Henderson, and Uchtred McDowell of French) who continued to
withhold "approved" status from Power at successive privy trials for nearly two years —
local resistance to a national ruling [entity:event/mr-gilbert-power-s-deposition-and-reposition].
