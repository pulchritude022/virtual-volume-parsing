# CH2/341/1 — Image 19 (folios 18v–19), 30 March 1643 — a second accuser

Left page (folio **18a**) records the presbytery's **answers to Power's demands**
(he stays suspended and the process must close before anything is revealed;
**Charles Campbell** and another are forborne as witnesses), then **Andrew McDowell
of Ellaster gives in a fresh accusation**. Right page (folio **19**) sets out
Ellaster's six articles — a near-echo of French's, adding **contemptuous
field-preaching** and Power's receiving of a **conformist minister-refugee from
Ireland** (his brother-in-law) into the covenant.

### Modern English (paraphrase)
[Power's third demand:] that the presbytery grant him a just copy and extract — of
the interrogatories to the witnesses and their depositions, and of the rest of the
process — under the clerk's hand, at his own charges. **The presbytery answers:**
to the first point (touching the ground of his suspension), **he continues
suspended till the process is closed**; to the second, **the process must be closed
before it is revealed**; to the third, it is fittest not considered till the process
closes. It was **debated whether Charles Campbell** (and another) should be received
as a witness in a particular of the accusation — the presbytery **forbears them**,
since the defender objected and the point concerned themselves.

**[Andrew of] Ellaster gives in a paper:** to the moderator and brethren, be it
known that — notwithstanding **[B.] McDowell younger of French** has already given
in an accusation against **Mr Gilbert Power** for perjury, usury, cloaking of
adultery and fornication, Sabbath-breaking, sedition, lying and many more crimes,
and though Power is suspended and "at the point of deposition" — yet since that
accusation Power has committed the following crimes, whereof **I, Andrew McDowell of
Ellaster, accuse him**…

## Folio 19 (right page)  *(confidence: medium)*  — Ellaster's six articles

### Diplomatic transcription

```
with the tennants of Portspittall complit [= settled] and reckoned with

yet he hes contemptouslie ever since preached publicklie[,] also
upon the [ ]nth of March or therby, preached upon

which I beleeve is more then any minister, much lesse

brother in law[.]  6[.] I can not but impart to your ws

of French, as followes[.] for notwithstanding the
laird of French, at the visitation of Stanikirk befor
the proces, offered to lay downe in money what [ ...]
```

### Modern English
**Ellaster's articles against Power:** **(1)** on (about) the last Sabbath of
September, drinking in **James Kirkpatrick's house** with the tenants of
**Portspittal**, settling and reckoning old debts and receiving their money;
accused before the Stoneykirk Session, he confessed the drinking, reckoning and
taking of money, and when charged with giving discharges said "he had neither pen
nor ink." **(2)** Sedition — his letter to the laird of Garthland (yet extant).
**(3)** Ordinary lying — (i) telling **Mr Alexander Turnbull** that he (French?)
"took bribes from the lairds of **Ardnell** and **Ellaster** to be his enemy"; (ii)
telling **John Baillie of Little Dunragit** that the process was willing to refer
French's whole accusation to Power's own oath. **(4)** He has **falsified the
Session book** in the Tarbret cloaking matter. **(5)** Notwithstanding his silencing,
he has contemptuously kept **preaching publicly** — about [mid-]March preaching **in
the fields beside his own dwelling**, morning and afternoon — and likewise **received
into the covenant a conformist minister who fled out of Ireland for fear of the
Irish** (a "confessor for conformity"), which is more than any minister, much less a
suspended one, might do — the said minister being **his brother-in-law**. **(6)** He
must also report Power's "inhumane dealing" with the laird of French: for
notwithstanding that the laird of French, at the Stoneykirk visitation before the
process, offered to lay down in money whatever…

## Notes & context
- **A second accuser.** **Andrew McDowell of Ellaster** joins **French (younger)**
  against Power, re-alleging the whole libel and adding **post-suspension** offences
  — proof the presbytery treats as aggravation. The anti-Power faction is now two
  McDowells (French and Ellaster), against the pro-Power petition of **George
  McCulloch** and six others (img_0017). The case is a **community split**.
- **New biographical thread for Power:** he is charged with **receiving into the
  covenant a conformist (Episcopalian) minister — his own brother-in-law — who fled
  Ulster "for fear of the Irish."** This adds family and confessional colour: Power
  had a minister brother-in-law caught in the 1641–42 Irish troubles.
- **The "usury/Sabbath" complex recurs:** reckoning tenants' debts and receiving
  their money **in the alehouse on the Sabbath** (James Kirkpatrick's house; the
  Portspittal tenants) — the same episode alluded to as "Knaggis's house" in
  img_0011; possibly two names for one event, worth reconciling.
- **Procedure:** the presbytery keeps Power suspended and the process sealed until
  closed; it **forbears Charles Campbell** (and another) as a witness because the
  defender objected and the matter concerned the witnesses themselves.
- **New people:** **Andrew McDowell of Ellaster** (2nd accuser), **James Kirkpatrick**,
  **John Baillie of Little Dunragit**, the **conformist minister / Power's
  brother-in-law**, the laird of **Ardnell (Ardwell)**.
- **Palaeography applied:** neat main-clerk hand (better legibility); `complit &
  reckoned` = settled accounts; `buds` = bribes; `conforme minister` / `confessour
  for conformitie` = an Episcopalian who conformed to bishops; `resave him in the
  covenant` = admit him to the National Covenant; `portrack` (uncertain) =
  dwelling/steading. Dates "1642" are Old Style. `y`+superscript → *th-*.
- **Lowest-confidence readings for the human reviewer (Dad):**
  1. **Andrew McDowell of Ellaster** — confirm the designation "Ellaster" (Elrig?
     a McDowell estate) and that he is a distinct person from French.
  2. The **conformist brother-in-law minister** who fled Ireland — a lead worth
     chasing (who was he? which Ulster parish?).
  3. `James Kirkpatrick` vs the earlier **Knaggis** — reconcile the Sabbath-drinking
     locus.
  4. French's forename (again "[B.]") remains unresolved.
  5. `portrack`, the mid-March preaching date, and article 6 (continues next opening).
