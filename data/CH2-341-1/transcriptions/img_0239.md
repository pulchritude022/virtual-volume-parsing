## Notes & context
★★★ **The Galloway regiment-chaplaincy — Stranraer volleys it back to Wigton.** The Earl of Galloway's repeated demand for a regimental minister (imgs 236–237) gets a firm, almost diplomatic reply: **"the Presbytery of Wigton ought to supply the first turn, and hereafter [we] shall show that we will not be wanting in what is incumbent for us to do."** Stranraer names **Mr John Crosse and Mr James Lawrie** to keep correspondence with Wigton and press them to send the first chaplain. A revealing bit of inter-presbytery buck-passing over the burden of army service — neither bounds eager to lose a minister to the field in the last weeks before Worcester.

★★ **John Campbell finally bends — "the overhaul of his process."** The notorious hold-out (imgs 216–238) at last **"gives some acknowledgment," admits "the knowledge of his offence," and asks that a brother confer with him to convince him.** The presbytery obliges generously: he may confer with **his own minister Mr John Park**, and if he satisfies before the Sabbath, the process proceeds; he is even granted **"the overhaul [review] of his process" at his own desire.** After months of contempt, a first real crack in his resistance.

★ **A ruinous kirk and defaulting heritors at Glenluce.** The **26 August visitation** finds the **Glenluce heritors again failing "their undertaking"** to meet about repairs; the presbytery, weighing "the ruinous condition of the fabric" and the session's diminished funds, resolves to press the repair "unanimously and cheerfully." The kirk-fabric thread (imgs 237–238) grinds on. A national **fast/warning** (from the Dundee Assembly, img 238) is again examined, and a **pro re nata is set for 17 September 1651.**
