The Rhins of Galloway look across a narrow sea to Ulster, and the volume is threaded through
with that closeness. **Ireland** is one of the most frequently named places in the whole
register — not as a foreign country but as a neighbour reachable by an afternoon's sail from
Portpatrick, in constant traffic of ministers, refugees, correspondence, and fugitives.

## Ministers across the water

Two of the presbytery's own leading men had deep Irish ties. **[Mr John
Livingstone](entity:person/mr-john-livingstone)** and **[Mr James
Blair](entity:person/mr-james-blair)** are repeatedly sent to, or associated with, the
ministry in Ireland, and the presbytery corresponds with Ulster brethren over the shared
business of the Scottish plantation churches. The 1640s Ulster covenant-subscription and the
cross-channel supply of pulpits recur as ordinary agenda items.

## Refugees and relief

The Irish Rising of 1641 and the wars that followed drove waves of **Protestant refugees**
across to Galloway, and the presbytery organises **relief for the Irish refugees** and deals
with conformist ministers arriving as fugitives from their own parishes. The North Channel
carries misery as well as commerce.

## The escape hatch of discipline

Ireland is also, again and again, where people under discipline **flee** — see
[Fugitives from discipline](entity:event/fugitives-from-discipline). An accused adulterer, a
deserting spouse, an excommunicate: the standard move is to "cross to Ireland," and the
presbytery's letters chasing them across the water are among the most human traces in the
book of just how porous this border really was.
