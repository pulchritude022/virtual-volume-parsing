### Modern English
The opening carries over from the previous page the examination of James Ross of Balneil: asked what had caused him to leave, he answered that when he had received word of the incoming of the English army he said he would not remove out of the kingdom until all the apparent danger of wrong and the first alarm were past. On being pressed further he stated that the quartering [rendezvous] was appointed at Dalgness[?], and from there he was to go along the Border side and so forward into Galloway.

The presbytery, having heard and considered Balneil's answers, remembering the witnesses' depositions, and comparing the article of the [Commission's] act with them — and finding that the act of the Commission "anent [those] to be debarred from the eldership," together with its applications, is **not fully plain** as to whether the said James Ross is to be debarred — resolves that, because it is necessary to know from the Commission's act (or its explanation) whether one who took the commission to Parliament and was sent therewith is to be debarred, or whether those who ought to be reponed [restored] are so according to those acts, the presbytery **refers his process to the Commission of the Kirk**, that it may be ruled upon by them. The clerk is to give one copy of the process to the commissioner going to the Commission, and another to James Ross of Balneil.

The concluding (badly faded) lines direct that when the ruling comes back it be brought to the next kirk day; if the parties differ, it is to be delayed and, once copied, they are to see themselves satisfied with the ruling. Mr John Crosse, who is going to Edinburgh, is appointed to attend the Commission of the Kirk with this matter.

## Notes & context
This opening **closes out the long Balneil affair** (running since img_148): rather than reaching a verdict itself, the presbytery finds that the **Commission of the Kirk's own act is "not fully plain"** on the crucial question — does a man's having accepted a *commission to Parliament* during the Engagement (and being sent with it) automatically debar him from the eldership, or may such men be reponed? — and therefore **refers James Ross of Balneil's whole process up to the Commission of the Kirk** for a ruling. The clerk is to give one copy of the process to the commissioner travelling to the Commission (**Mr John Crosse**, going to Edinburgh) and another to Balneil himself. This is the procedural terminus of the dossier assembled across imgs 152–161: the witnesses had cleared him of pressing the Engagement, but his having taken the parliamentary commission was the one point on which the Kirk's purge-machinery was ambiguous, so it goes to the centre.

★ **Covenant-renewal subscription business:** the ruling elder of [Chalmerain?] raises how the renewed profession/subscription is to be enforced "upon every ruling man," and the presbytery is tasked to draw up the terms per the General Assembly's act — the ground-level administration of the 1648–49 Covenant renewal reaching the parishes.

★ **Parish-boundary adjustment desired by my Lord Cassillis (the elder):** a part of [Kirkinner?] parish is to be considered for annexation to [Cassuoch?], and the presbytery will meet on-site to **perambulate the bounds** — a physical walking of the march, "for the people's ease" (i.e. so parishioners are not too far from their kirk). The Earl of Cassillis (a leading Kirk-party nobleman, recurring throughout this volume) is the mover.

Penance updates (routine): **James Neilson** received after satisfying for adultery, his partner still on the way of satisfaction; **James Halyburton and Grissell Robertson** likewise on the way of satisfaction. The **Andrew Cowan irregular-baptism case** (running since img_150/156 — the Mochrum baptism "by a stranger minister") is again delayed, with Cowan now to be summoned as a **witness**. Next meeting appointed for **the 20th**, Mr John Crosse to make the exercise.
