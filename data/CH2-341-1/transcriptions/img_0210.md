### Modern English
… [Mr Andrew Stuart], who was the minister while he lived in Donaghadee, answered **he would never seek one out from him; but if he had anything to lay to his charge, he would answer it.** And being thus further required, **under the pain of ecclesiastical censure, to procure a testimonial under the minister's or session's own hand — whereby the presbytery might rest satisfied and he might be released — he replied, "I will send myself to never seek it while I breathe."** And at last, when it was signified to him that the end of requiring the testimonial was to take away the suspicion of his accession to any malignant courses (where he "lifted the custom" in Donaghadee), **he answered, "What! malignancy in me!" and, turning his back disdainfully and putting on his cap, said he would never come more in that matter, do what they please.** **The presbytery, taking his misbehaviour into consideration, accounted it a great contempt of the judicatory, and appoints that his behaviour be recorded as it is expressed; and, calling for him again, he appeared, and his misdemeanour being represented to him, he was summoned *apud acta* to appear before the Synod next Tuesday — which he would not obey.**

## Notes & context
★★★ **"What! malignancy in me!" — a defiant refusal, recorded verbatim.** A wonderfully vivid disciplinary scene: **Robert Campbell**, required to produce a testimonial of his conduct in **Donaghadee** (Ulster), openly defies the presbytery. He says he would **"never seek one out"** from his former minister (Mr Andrew Stuart); required again "under pain of ecclesiastical censure," he retorts **"I will send myself to never seek it while I breathe"**; and when told the point was to clear him of "accession to malignant courses" (he had **"lifted the custom" [collected duties/excise] in Donaghadee**), he bursts out **"What! malignancy in me!" and, "turning his back disdainfully and putting on his cap,"** storms off. The presbytery **records his very words as "a great contempt of the judicatory"** and summons him to the Synod — which he also refuses. A rare, novelistic capture of a layman's face-to-face rebellion against the kirk-court, and of how the 1650 malignancy-vetting of Ireland-returned men could curdle into open confrontation.

★★ **The war-crime allegation firms up.** On **John Adair of Maryport's** eyewitness testimony, **"John Campbell was the man who apprehended and beat Knockeghinnoch"** (to force him into George Munro's army, img 207) — with **Robert Campbell's daughter** cited as a corroborating witness. John Campbell fails to compear; the case is held over. The Ireland/Ulster malignancy thread (imgs 204–210) is now a running docket of hard, named cases.

★ **The Balneil appeal — housekeeping.** The presbytery declines to transcribe **Balneil's long "observations"** into the register (the appeal-reasons "contain the substance of it"), reserving the right to call for it from the **General Assembly clerk** if the case reopens (cf. imgs 200–202). Routine: the common head *De Ecclesia visibili instituta* disputed; Dick away at the **Commission of the Kirk**; Arisemman still sick; correspondence with the **Synod of Glasgow**.
