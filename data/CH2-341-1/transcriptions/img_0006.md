### Modern English
"…for we had nothing for it, but were beholden to give it as the rest of the
barony." Asked whether Mr Gilbert broke up his barn door and got the aforesaid
corn out of it, he answers no: Mr Gilbert was at the other end of the barn at
first; his (Mr Gilbert's) woman came and [raised?] some out; the door was opened
between them; the barn door would not open, it was but 6 or 7 years old; his
woman winnowed the corn, and then his own wife, being afield, came home and [gave]
it to them. Mr Gilbert came and saw it received, but was not there when the door
was opened. Asked what he knew about the [bell?], he answers he knew there was one
but did not know what became of it.

**The presbytery gives its answers to Mr Gilbert's last paper.** To the first
point: the reasons that are in the presbytery book he may have if he seek them,
but not those given in to the Synod (the presbytery kept no copy, having given
them to the Synod clerk). To the second: the presbytery delays to answer until
all the remaining witnesses are examined. To the third, concerning Mr Joseph
Cleland: it is answered that French holds his deposition under his hand to give
in; and as to Mr George Galloway and Mr George Kinhilt[?], the presbytery thinks
it improper to examine witnesses not given in by the accuser — for if nothing be
proven against a man he is to be esteemed innocent, and they cannot compel
witnesses to swear contradictions; but if any of the said [parties] scruple, they
may seek information from the persons themselves. The fourth is granted. The
fifth is referred to the close of the depositions.

The presbytery appoints French, before Saturday next, to send the clerk the
particulars of the whole libel.

### Modern English
[The particulars] which he (French) hath not yet given in are to be sent by the
clerk to Mr Gilbert, and Mr Gilbert to give an answer thereto the next presbytery
day. The presbytery appoints the clerk to give out summonses for the witnesses
that have not yet compeared. The next meeting is appointed this day fortnight; Mr
Hugh Hutcheson to make the exercise and Mr John Porterfield to add. Mr Alexander
Robison exercised ably on "Seek ye first the kingdom of God" [Matthew 6:33], and
was for the most part approved, only exhorted about some small matters.

**Presbyterial meeting at Stranraer on Wednesday 17 November 1642.** The exercise
was made by Mr Hugh Hutcheson and approved for what was delivered; the second
speaker, Mr John Porterfield, absent, and the Ruling Elders of Portpatrick and
Leswalt absent.

When some commissions in writing from several kirk sessions, [appointing] ruling
elders to sit in the presbytery, were given in, Mr Gilbert Power protested against
**Garthland's commission** — which was written and subscribed by Mr Gilbert's own
hand — saying he ought not to be judged in his action because he (Garthland) is a
party. The presbytery, having formerly discussed that exception, approves the
commission. Mr Gilbert also protested against **Charles Campbell's commission**,
saying he was a party; the commission is approved by all but one.

**French gives in a paper**, the tenor whereof follows: "The substance of the
particulars whereof I first accuse Mr Gilbert, contained in the general heads of
the libel, which are not yet instanced: (1) whereas I accuse him of **cloaking
adultery**, instanced already; (2) whereas I accuse him of **usury**, instanced
already…"

## Notes & context

Left page (folio **5a**) finishes Patrick Wilson's barn-door deposition, then
records the **presbytery's point-by-point answer** to Mr Gilbert Power's
supplication, and orders French (the accuser) to give in the particulars of the
whole libel. Right page (folio **6**) opens a new sederunt — **Stranraer,
Wednesday 17 November 1642** — with Power's objections to two ruling-elder
**commissions** (he says the elders are parties and he ought not be judged by
them), and then **French's paper of charges**, whose general heads finally name
the offences: **cloaking adultery** and **usury**.
- **The charges named at last.** French's paper (folio 6) gives the "general
  heads" of the libel against **Mr Gilbert Power**: **cloaking adultery**
  (concealing/covering someone's adultery — a serious offence for a minister) and
  **usury** (usurie). These tie together the earlier evidence: the annualrent on
  the McDowall loan (img_0002), the corn/oppression testimony (img_0005), the
  500-merk bond and Peter McCulloch's testament (img_0003–0004) all bear on the
  usury charge.
- **Recusal fight over ruling elders.** Power tried to disqualify two elder
  commissions — **Garthland's** and **Charles Campbell's** — arguing the elders are
  *parties* and cannot judge him. The presbytery over-ruled both (Garthland's
  approved by all; Campbell's "by all but one"). Note the irony flagged by the
  scribe: Garthland's commission "was written & subscribed by Mr Gilbert's own
  hand."
- **Presbytery's answer to the supplication** (folio 5v) is careful procedure: it
  refuses to examine witnesses not led by the accuser (presumption of innocence;
  "they cannot compel witnesses to swear contradictions"), grants the 4th demand,
  and defers the rest.
- **New sederunt: Stranraer, Wed 17 Nov 1642.** Exercise by **Mr Hugh Hutcheson**;
  **Mr Alexander Robison** had exercised (at the previous meeting) on Matthew 6:33.
- **Palaeography applied:** `cloaking adulterie` = concealing adultery; `usurie` =
  usury; `commissioun` = a session's written appointment of a ruling elder;
  `instanced` = specified with particulars/instances; `exercise` = the set
  expository sermon at a presbytery meeting; `subscryvit` = subscribed/signed;
  `wonsday` = Wednesday; `befor` = against/by (a date). `y`+superscript → *th-*.
