### Modern English
> … **[the answer being] delayed — used to my great prejudice — yet I trust your Wisdoms not only anew to remit me to the Commission of the Kirk, but also [consider] that there have been added, since January 1649, to my process divers acts and allegations; and some thereof omitted, and others wherein I have not been arraigned; whereby I may incur great prejudice, in regard of some mistakes (as I conceive) and other additions and allegations, against which I might have objected and ought to have been heard, and now report the same. That first your Wisdoms will be pleased to advise and to have me heard: wherefore I humbly beseech your Wisdoms that the foresaid acts, additions, and other allegations since January 1649 may be taken to consideration and verified, omissions added, and my answers joined to the allegations, that so the process may be clear and perfect; and your Wisdoms' answer. Thus subscribed, James Ross.**

**The presbytery, taking this last supplication to consideration, desired that James Ross of Balneil would condescend upon those things alleged to be added to, or omitted in, that process, and the other allegations wherewith he had not been arraigned.** **The said James, having received a copy of the presbytery's Declaration upon his supplication, required time to prepare to instruct the foresaid additions and omissions.** **Whereunto the presbytery answered that, provided it should be certified that none did retard or hinder him in his going to the Commission of the Kirk, they were content he should have time till next meeting to instruct the process — at which time the presbytery resolves to meet at the kirk of Inch.**

## Notes & context
★★★ **Two supplications of James Ross of Balneil — verbatim — and a legalistic defence.** The volume's central penitent puts his case on the record in his own words (imgs 197–198). The **first** supplication is a plea for readmission to the Solemn League and Covenant "according to the ordinance of the late General Assembly … that I, having the scandal removed, may enjoy the benefit of the Kirk and the fellowship of good men." The **second** is a striking piece of **lay legal self-defence**: Balneil complains that **since January 1649 "divers acts and allegations" have been added to his process, some omitted, and others on which he "has not been arraigned,"** to his prejudice; he demands the whole process **"be taken to consideration and verified, omissions added, and my answers joined to the allegations, that so the process may be clear and perfect."** The presbytery — his case having been **remitted up to the Commission of the Kirk** — declines to meddle unless it is referred back, but gives him **time till the next meeting (at Inch) to "instruct" his objections, provided no one is hindering his going to the Commission.** A rare, fully-documented instance of a delated malignant contesting the *procedure* of his own kirk trial.

★★ **Commissioners to the General Assembly of July 1650.** The presbytery elects **Mr John Dick, Mr John Crosse, and Mr John Park, with the Sheriff as ruling elder,** to the **General Assembly at Edinburgh, the second Wednesday of July 1650** — the last GA before Cromwell's conquest disrupted the Kirk's supreme court. A datable national fingerprint.

★ **The Inch planting dispute sharpens.** The clerk is ordered to write the Inch parish a pointed letter: they are **"exceedingly far behind in performing their undertakings"** and **negligent "in using means to retain their minister [Mr John Dick] among them."**
