Glenluce is the single most persistent piece of unfinished business in the whole volume —
a parish whose vacancy, funding dispute, and eventual split into two charges runs, in one
form or another, from the earliest folios through to the 1649 material. It is as much a
story about money (a mortification tied to the ruined **Glenluce Abbey**) as about
ministry.

## An early false start (1642)

Even before the Power case dominates the record, **James Rosse of Balneil** tries to
present **Mr James Smith** to Glenluce on the strength of a 1000-merk mortification linked
to the Abbey ruins. The presbytery rejects the presentation, asserting itself "undoubted
patron," and instead tries to arrange Glenluce's provision through **Mr George Dick**
[img_0044–0045] — a plan that goes nowhere. Glenluce sits vacant, supplied by a rotating
cast of the presbytery's own ministers, for years.

## The General Assembly splits the parish (1646)

By 1646 the impasse is referred all the way up to the **General Assembly**, which orders
Glenluce **divided into two charges**: the **old kirk of Glenluce**, funded from "the late
mortification," and a **new kirk erected at Balneil** (= New Luce), funded from the old
420-merk local stipend, with a royal provision of 1000 merks, the Abbey teinds, and 200
merks for a schoolmaster [img_106]. The heritors split over the act — Balneil, John
McDowell, and John Adair of Genoch consenting; Thomas Hay, a Kennedy of Ardgirvane, and John
Baillie of Imrogart dissenting over their share of the 380 merks earmarked for **Mr George
Dick** [img_107] — and the presbytery moves to sue the dissenters for arrears through the
Lords of Session [img_111].

## Two ministers, at last (1647–1649)

**Mr John Crichton** accepts the call to the new kirk (Balneil/New Luce) and is admitted
in 1647, secured by a bond from Balneil for an 800-merk stipend [img_108, img_113–115]. The
old kirk takes longer: **Mr John Scott** is finally presented, admitted without objection at
the kirk door, and given formal possession of the manse and glebe — the whole presbytery
walking out to **Glenluce Abbey** together for the occasion — only in March 1648
[img_126–127]. Even after both charges are filled, the glebe and manse of the new kirk
remain unsettled into the following year, with Balneil himself as the counterparty in a
running property dispute that **Mr James Blair** and **Mr John Crosse** are deputed to
settle [img_143, img_148, img_157].

## Why it matters for the wiki

Glenluce's slow planting is the throughline that connects several of this volume's other
storylines: it is why [James Ross of Balneil](entity:person/james-ross-of-balneil) appears
constantly across two decades of the register (as patron, bondsman, and later the accused
in his own [malignancy process](entity:event/james-ross-of-balneil-s-malignancy-process));
it is why **Mr George Dick** and **Mr John Scott** recur so often; and it is a useful case
study in how a Covenanter presbytery actually got a vacant, financially entangled parish
staffed — patiently, over years, with the civil courts as a backstop.
