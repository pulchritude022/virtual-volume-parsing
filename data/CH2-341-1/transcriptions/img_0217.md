## Notes & context
★★★ **The Maryport deposition closes — Patrick Adair, and the Ulster kin-network.** The tail of the sworn deposition (img 216) delivers two striking details. First, **Knockeghinnoch's defiance mid-assault** — "though you should take my life" — and John Campbell's claim of kinship ("this is my mother's brother"), sketching the tangled family loyalties of the Ulster-Scots planters. Second, and notably, **"Patrick Adair, intending for Scotland to make the [matter] good"** — very likely **the Rev. Patrick Adair** (c.1624–1694), the leading minister of the Presbyterian church in Ulster and its historian, here caught in a Wigtownshire parish record carrying the affair to Scotland. The deposition closes with the formal **extract-subscription of the session clerk** — a fully registered legal instrument.

★★ **The censure of the Ireland malignants advances.** With the deposition entered, the presbytery notes **John Campbell "not yet to be found within the kingdom"** (still absent, cf. imgs 212–214), while **Robert Campbell now compears and undertakes** to fetch a **testimonial of his carriage in Ireland** — exactly the condition the presbytery set under threat of suspension (img 214). The Ulster-malignancy process is now producing sworn evidence and submission.

★ **The robbery case comes to proof.** The long-running charge that **Thomas Baillie of Old Glenluce robbed a "poor Englishman"** (imgs 184, 205, 212) reaches the witness stage: Baillie raises no objection to the witnesses; **Thomas Nisbet swears he knew nothing of it**; and **David Kennedy of [Killalie]** begins a fuller account (Baillie escorting the Englishman toward Stranraer). Also: the **Inch glebe possession** for Mr John Dick is again pressed (cf. imgs 214–215), and **George Adestoun** gets his first admonition.

  - Right folio 217 is clearer; the robbery-witness readings (Nisbet, Kennedy, "father's house," "poor Englishman") are moderately secure.
