Among the sins the presbytery policed was one that reaches into the heart of everyday economic
life: **usury** — the taking of unlawful interest ("annualrent") on money lent. The register's
handful of usury cases open a small window onto the 17th-century **moral economy**, in which
lending, debt, and profit were matters of conscience the church claimed the right to judge.

## Lawful and unlawful annualrent

The distinction the court works with is not that interest is forbidden outright but that it
must be **lawful** — the era recognised a legal ceiling (an annualrent of around eight per
cent), above which lending became the sin of usury. Accusations of usury therefore turn on
concrete sums and rates, and defences argue that the interest taken was within the lawful
bound. The volume's opening business already includes such a charge, entangled with the
broader libel of the period [img_6, img_8].

## Oppression and the powerful

Usury shades into the court's wider concern with **oppression** — the exploitation of the
weak by the strong, whether a creditor squeezing a debtor, a landlord a widow, or an engrosser
the market. The kirk's claim to discipline these behaviours asserted a vision of economic life
as answerable to the same moral order as sexual or Sabbath conduct.

## Why it matters

These cases are rare but revealing: they show a church court adjudicating the ethics of money
in a small agrarian society, and they preserve specific financial dealings — loans, rates,
debts — that would otherwise leave no trace.
