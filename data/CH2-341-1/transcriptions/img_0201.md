## Notes & context
★★★ **James Ross of Balneil's appeal to the General Assembly — his eight "reasons," verbatim.** This is the documentary climax of the volume's central case: **Balneil's formal appeal (from the Presbytery of Stranraer to the July 1650 General Assembly)**, entered into the register in his own words. His grounds are a striking piece of mid-17th-century lay legal argument: (1) his own repeated supplications "since 1648" for trial were shelved by the "protracting" of his process; (2) the extract given him for the Commission differs (with additions and omissions) from the earlier acts; (3) he was delayed "beyond all example," worse than men who actually **"went on in the Engagement, and were in England and Stirling"**; (4) **"of all the members that were on the Committee, I only was arraigned for the acts at Kirkcowan"** — a claim of selective prosecution; (5) the "great informality of the process" and biased "carriage" of members; (6) members "professing prejudice and threatening me"; (7) the delays "carried on of purpose … some party making use of their specious pretext of malignancy … to the detriment of my name and estate"; (8) "very eminent members of the presbytery have been my [adversaries]." **A remarkable articulation of a delated malignant's claim that a Covenanting kirk-court was being used as an instrument of local faction against his person and estate.** (The presbytery's answer to these reasons follows in subsequent openings.)

★ **Kirk business around the appeal.** The **planting of Mr John Dick at Inch** ("Mr John Dick's transportation") is still before the Synod's committee, and the **Inch parish is pressed one last time** to satisfy before the General Assembly; correspondence continues with **Wigton over the trial of Mr William Thomson** (adjourned).
