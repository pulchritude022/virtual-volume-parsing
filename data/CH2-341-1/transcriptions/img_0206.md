## Notes & context
★★★ **The Rhins reacts to Dunbar — "the sad stroke from the hand of the Lord upon the land."** This *pro re nata* sitting is dated **4 September 1650 — the day after the catastrophic Covenanter defeat at Dunbar (3 September 1650).** The presbytery's language is unmistakable: **"considering the sad stroke from the hand of the Lord upon the land … in this day of gloom … the people should be stirred up to humble themselves,"** calling for public humiliation in the Sabbath meetings and private humiliation "apart in their families." And, war pressing on the Rhins itself, the brethren **exhort "the Colonel and the Committee within the bounds of this presbytery (now residing at Stranraer) to use all possible diligence for going out in an orderly way"** — i.e. to muster and march — the Committee promising "to do what was possible." A first-rank local reaction to Dunbar, days after the battle; note even the *ordinary* meeting failed **"by reason of the violence of the storm."**

★★ **The building of Mr John Dick's manse at Inch.** The Inch settlement moves to bricks and mortar: a committee of ministers and elders (with **"some skilful workmen"**) is to fix **"the place whereon Mr John Dick's dwelling may be built"** — the vacancy (imgs 191–204) now producing a manse.

★ **The Gilbert Kennedy adultery scandal spreads; a Munro-forces penitent.** The **adultery case against Gilbert Kennedy of Aviolen** widens to a cluster of women — **Margaret Wilson** (img 205, "followed Gilbert to Edinburgh"), **Jonet McMillan** of Inch (who denies), **Jonet Semple**, and another — parcelled out to the sessions of Inch and Stranraer. And **Alexr Parker**, who **served/foraged under George Munro's forces "in that malignant course,"** supplicates for readmission — his case referred to the Stranraer session (cf. img 205).
