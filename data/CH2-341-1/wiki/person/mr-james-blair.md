Minister first of **Portpatrick**, later admitted to **Kirkcolm** (3 January 1644, ending a
long vacancy there) — and, across the volume, one of Mr Gilbert Power's few open partisans.

## Defending Power, and being attacked for it

Blair supplicates the presbytery to clear his own name after **Uchtred McDowell of French**
accuses him of defaming French as "injurious, wicked, an unconscionable dealer" — fallout
from Blair's support of Power. The presbytery arbitrates and finds no ground in French's
paper against him [img_0037–0040]. It is a rare case in this register of a minister being
drawn into another man's discipline case purely by association.

## A repeated traveller to Ireland

Like Livingstone, Blair spends real stretches of the 1640s in Ireland — noted as "gone to
Ireland" alongside Livingstone in 1648 [img_136] and again in the mid-1640s [img_71] — part
of the presbytery's ongoing pastoral relief effort for the Ulster Scots community, on top of
his own charge.

## A key witness in the Balneil case

Decades later, Blair resurfaces as one of the ministers whose personal testimony clears
**James Ross of Balneil** of malignancy: Blair saw Balneil subscribe the National Covenant at
the Glenluce gentry-binding and privately heard him call the Engagement unlawful — testimony
central to Balneil's eventual vindication [img_161,
entity:event/james-ross-of-balneil-s-malignancy-process]. He is also, at an earlier stage of
that same process, the lone dissenting voice arguing the case was "not ripe enough" for a
verdict [img_159] — a glimpse of a careful, minority-minded juror rather than a rubber stamp.
