### Modern English

### Modern English
… [the dittays] given in against those under the scandal of witchcraft.

**Presbytery meeting at Inch, 1 July [1650].** All the brethren present; all the ruling elders absent except those of Kirkcolm and Inch. **The dittays given in against Issobell Murdoch, Jonet Dalyell, Jonet Paterson, Katherine McKennay, Jonet Aitken, and Jonet Lindsay — parishioners of Old Glenluce and Leswalt — were read in the hearing of the presbytery; as also the several dittays against Agnes Little, who had no certain residence in any parish. **The presbytery, being informed that John Fouler in [Inch] had insolently fallen on another man, to the effusion of his blood, and robbed him of what he had, appointed him to be cited to the next meeting; and Mr James Blair to represent the same to the Sheriff.** **The presbytery, having received a letter from the Commission of the Kirk for keeping a solemn humiliation** (in regard there was great cause), resolved it should be kept next Thursday, intimation having been given to the people the Sabbath before. **Mr Alexr Turnbull and Mr James Lawrie report they had attended the Presbytery of Wigton** …

## Notes & context
★★★ **A mass witchcraft commission — six named women plus Agnes Little.** The 1650 witch-hunt reaches its peak in this presbytery. **Formal dittays (indictments) are given in and read against a whole cluster of accused: Issobell Murdoch, Jonet Dalyell, Jonet Paterson, Katherine McKennay, Jonet Aitken, and Jonet Lindsay — parishioners of Old Glenluce and Leswalt — plus the vagrant Agnes Little ["Green-bittle", img 195].** The presbytery **finds the articles "relevant"** (i.e. legally sufficient) **"that a commission may be sought thereupon,"** and **recommends it to the commissioners going to the July General Assembly.** This is exactly the mechanism by which the great 1649–50 Scottish witch-panic produced local commissions of justiciary — a named group of women moving from kirk-session delation to presbytery dittay to petition for a trial commission. A centrally important page for the volume's witchcraft thread; the six names should seed the wiki's persons index and be watched for outcomes.

★★ **Balneil appeals to the General Assembly.** The Balneil malignancy case escalates one more rung: his written **"observations" on his process** (alleging that acts and allegations were added or altered — cf. img 198) are judged to **"reflect bitterly upon the judicatory"** and rejected; the presbytery stands on its register and declines to reopen the process. **Balneil then verbally appeals to the July 1650 General Assembly**, and Turnbull is set to draft the presbytery's answer to his appeal. The volume's central penitent is now fighting his case up to the Kirk's supreme court.

★ **A solemn humiliation, and an assault.** A **letter from the Commission of the Kirk appoints a solemn humiliation** (fast) for "next Thursday" — the counterpart to the June thanksgiving (img 194); in the anxious summer of 1650 the Kirk oscillates between thanksgiving and humiliation. And **John Fouler in Inch** is cited for a bloody assault-and-robbery ("insolently fallen on another man, to the effusion of his blood").
