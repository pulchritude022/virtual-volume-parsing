Beneath the doctrinal storms, the presbytery was also a welfare institution, and one of the
most persistent kinds of business in the whole volume is the care of the **poor, and of the
widows and orphans of war.** As the fighting of the 1640s drained men from the Rhins, the
register fills with collections, rolls, and disbursements for those left behind.

## Rolls of widows and orphans

The presbytery repeatedly draws up and calls in **lists of war widows and orphans** across
its parishes, allocating relief money to them from collections and mortifications. These rolls
are, incidentally, a small census of the human cost of the wars in one corner of Scotland —
named women and children, parish by parish, whose men did not come home.

## Collections upon collections

The decade brings a stream of charitable gatherings routed through the court: for **Irish
refugees**, for the town of **Dundee** after its storm, for **Argyll**, for prisoners taken
at [Dunbar](entity:event/the-dunbar-prisoners-relief), and for the ordinary parish poor. Each
is administered the same careful way — called in from the sessions, accounted, and forwarded —
and together they show a small institution acting as a redistribution network across a wide
and often desperate bounds.

## Mortifications and the poor's box

Longer-term relief ran on **mortifications** (endowments) and the parish poor's box, and the
volume includes disputes over their proper use — including the recovery of poor-relief money
that had been withheld or misapplied. The care of the poor was not sentimental; it was
audited, litigated, and enforced like any other charge.

## Why it matters

For anyone tracing ordinary lives, these are among the richest pages: they name the people
the grander narrative usually omits — the widowed, the orphaned, the destitute — and record
what a Scottish community actually did for them.
